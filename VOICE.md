# VOICE.md — Trevor Burkholder Narration Voice

## Core Principle

Trust the cut. Don’t explain what you just showed. The reader fills the gap — let them.

-----

## Sentence Architecture

**Short declaratives carry the weight.**
Let them land before moving to the next thing.
“Disaster avoided.” is a complete sentence. It earns its period.

**Conditional constructions give players agency without breaking immersion.**
“As you take to a table, if you take to a table” — this is the voice offering the world without dictating the character’s response. Use this sparingly; when it lands it lands hard.

**The mundane earns the meaningful.**
A cabbage falling off a cart gets the same attention as documents exchanging hands in an alley. The equivalence is the point. The world doesn’t editorialize about which thing matters. The player decides.

-----

## Emotion: State It, Don’t Perform It

Never describe the emotional atmosphere. Describe the physical reality and let the emotion be implied.

❌ “A cold dread settles over the party as the weight of what they’ve just done becomes clear.”
✓ “You don’t feel the normal elation brought by a hard won victory.”

The second version trusts the reader. It also assumes familiarity — “normal elation” implies this party has won before, has a baseline. That’s characterization embedded in restraint.

**“Perhaps” is a precision tool.**
“Perhaps the cost of merely getting to this fight was too steep.”
It offers the interpretation without forcing it. The player may have a different read. That’s the point.

-----

## Sensory Detail

Lead with the unexpected sense.
The tavern scene opens with sound — the quiet, then the lamps, then the return of activity.
The night watch scene opens with smell — sweet air, floral fragrance — before the eye picks up the vendor and the alley.

Unexpected sense first destabilizes the familiar. The player is present before they know why.

**Specific > general.**
“A produce vendor” not “a man.”
“A cabbage” not “something.”
“Buxom young brunette” not “a woman.”
The specificity is the immersion.

-----

## Endings

The last line does the work. Everything before it is setup.

“A problem for tomorrow or an investigation right now?” — this is the entire purpose of the scene delivered in one line. No preamble. No explanation of what hangs in the balance. The player knows.

“You’ll know it when you see it.” — four words of mystery delivered without ceremony and immediately abandoned. The messenger doesn’t linger for effect. Neither does the narration.

**Don’t waste the landing.**
Cut anything in the final sentence that doesn’t need to be there.
If you can feel the writer checking whether the reader understood, cut it.

-----

## Pacing

**The mundane gets breath.**
The cabbage sequence — fall, run, retrieve, cart escaping, caught — each beat gets its moment. This is not padding. This is the world being real before it becomes plot.

**Then the cut.**
“Down an alley you see documents being exchanged for money.”
No transition. No “but then.” The world just contains both things simultaneously and the eye moves between them.

The rhythm: breathe, breathe, cut.

-----

## What This Voice Never Does

- Explains the implications of what it just described
- Uses adjectives the noun already implies
- Adds a sentence to make sure the reader understood the previous sentence
- Lingers on atmosphere beyond what a single sensory beat requires
- Tells the player how to feel
- Editorializes on which details matter
- Signals that something important is happening before the important thing happens

-----

## The Underlying Philosophy

This voice assumes the player is intelligent and present.
It gives them the world and gets out of the way.
The GM is not performing — the GM is transmitting.

Short. Specific. Trust the cut.
