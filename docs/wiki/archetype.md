From Jungian psychology. A structural tendency in the psyche to organize experience in recurring patterns: the Hero, the [Shadow](/wiki/shadow/), the Mother, the Trickster, and others.

An archetype is not a specific image. It is the underlying pattern that different cultures and people express through different symbols. The Hero is not one character from one mythology. It is a pattern of meaning-making that appears wherever someone faces the unknown, overcomes it, and returns changed.

What appears in any given person, the specific images and figures, is shaped by culture, history, and individual experience. Two people can carry the same archetypal material and express it through entirely different symbols. They share the structure, not the surface.

[Resonance vectors](/wiki/resonance-vector/) connect to archetypal structures, not to symbols themselves. This is why a symbol that produces strong [Resonance](/wiki/resonance/) in one person does not produce the same response in another, and why the responses are not random. The symbol is the form through which the archetype is currently expressing itself.

Myth-OS does not require accepting Jung's full theoretical framework. The system works whether or not archetypes exist as Jung described them. The practice depends only on the observable fact that resonance responses occur and stay consistent over time.
