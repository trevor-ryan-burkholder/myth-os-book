From [Internal Family Systems](https://ifs-institute.com/) (IFS) therapy. A part that carries emotional pain, fear, shame, or grief from early experiences that were not adequately processed.

Exiles are typically held inaccessible by [protector systems](/wiki/protector/). The material they carry is painful and often destabilizing, so the rest of the system works to keep them contained. They are not gone. They are protected against, often effectively.

In Myth-OS, exiles correspond to [presences](/wiki/presence/) in the more defended areas of [The space](/wiki/the-space/) that produce strong protective responses when approached. The same underlying entity, named differently in different frameworks.

The two frameworks diverge on what to do with exiles. IFS includes an unburdening process, in which an exile's burden is symbolically released with the support of the Self and witnessing parts. That process requires a trained practitioner and a relational container. Myth-OS does not include unburdening. It builds the conditions under which presences become accessible, stable, observable, approachable, without forcing any particular outcome. More conservative in what it attempts. More applicable outside a clinical relationship as a result.

Somatically, exiles often register as contraction, heaviness, or a specific quality of pain in particular locations in the body.
