The process of moving attention toward a [Presence](/wiki/presence/) in [The space](/wiki/the-space/) without destabilizing it.

Correct approach is characterized by stable attention without leaning, present but not pushing. The system responds to the pressure of approach, not to its intent. Curiosity produces the same defensive response as hostility if both are applied with force. Demand does not require aggression. It is present any time you expect something to happen, wait for a response, or try to make what you see clearer than it currently is.

Expectation is the most common source of invisible pressure. You notice something and hold the expectation that it will do something, shift, respond, reveal itself further. That expectation changes how you are attending. You are no longer observing. You are waiting for a result, which is a form of demand. The [Presence](/wiki/presence/) responds to the demand. What was visible becomes less defined, or disappears.

What approach requires is a particular quality of attention: present but not leaning. Your position stays fixed. If the [Presence](/wiki/presence/) shifts location, you do not follow it. If it becomes less clear, you do not try to sharpen it. You hold where you are and observe what remains. The [Somatic channel](/wiki/somatic-channel/) often registers leaning before the mind does, a forward quality in the chest, a pulling sensation, a change in breathing. Catching it there gives you more room to hold the approach correctly.

In a [fragmented system](/wiki/working-with-a-fragmented-system/), approach starts further back than feels necessary. Any movement toward a defended [Protector](/wiki/protector/) begins from a distance that produces no response at all.
