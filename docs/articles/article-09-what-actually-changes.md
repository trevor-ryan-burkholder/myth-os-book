The most common expectation at the start of this kind of work is that something will go away.

The signals that cause the most trouble will stop activating. The reactions that wreck the most situations will be replaced by better ones. The versions of yourself that appear under specific conditions and behave in ways the rest of you would not choose, those will resolve into something more unified.

That expectation is wrong. Getting clear on what does and does not change is necessary for using the system correctly over time, and for not abandoning it when it produces results that do not match what you expected.

---

The signals do not go away. The presences that generate them stay in the space. Difficult situations continue to occur. Reactions still happen, sometimes at full strength, sometimes at the worst possible moment. The system does not remove any of this.

What it changes is narrower. Your position relative to what is happening, and what that position makes available.

Position is not a metaphor. It refers to how much a signal is running you automatically versus how much separation you have from it.

At the start, a signal activates and the response follows immediately. There is no gap. Signal and reaction are a single event. You are not observing the reaction. You are inside it before awareness has a chance to register what is occurring.

What the system builds, through repeated observation and contact under stable conditions, is a small amount of space between those two things. The signal still activates. The reaction still occurs. But there is a point, brief, partial, sometimes only a few seconds wide, where the activation is visible before the reaction is complete. That point is where something different becomes possible. Not control. Not override. Not a better response replacing the automatic one. Only the gap where previously there was none.

The signal still happens. You are no longer fully inside it.

---

The markers of this shift are not dramatic. They are consistent once you know what to look for.

The first is earlier detection. A signal that used to register only in retrospect, after the reaction had already finished, starts registering closer to activation. The reaction may still complete. The recognition preceded it rather than followed it, which changes the information available in the moment. A few seconds of lead time is a real change in what that moment contains.

You are in a conversation. Something the other person says produces an immediate tightening in the chest. A somatic signal you recognize from sessions, from previous situations with a similar quality. You are already responding before you have caught it consciously. The body registered it first, and that registration gives you two seconds you would not otherwise have had. The response continues, but slightly less at full force. You say something you might still regret, but not the version you would have said without the adjustment.

That is a valid result.

The second marker is reduced intensity. Reactions still occur but feel less consuming, not because the signal is producing less charge, but because the relationship to it has shifted. Some of it is being observed rather than experienced. The peak is lower. The duration is shorter. The aftermath is lighter.

The third is response flexibility. When a gap exists between signal and completion, that gap contains the possibility of a slightly different choice. Not a considered decision. A small variation in how the reaction expresses. You still react. You react differently, some of the time. In a situation that would have produced a sharp response, what appears instead is a slightly less sharp one. From the inside, the difference between one option and two is significant, even when the second is only marginally better than the first.

---

The short-term markers are visible within weeks to months. The longer arc is different in character.

At six months of consistent practice, the most frequently encountered signals are detectable early enough that real-time use is more reliable. Not consistent. Reliable. The gap is there often enough to stop being a surprise. The space is accessible with less preparation. Contact that required careful setup earlier is available with less overhead.

What has not changed at six months: signals still activate at full strength. The most defended presences are still defended. New situations the practice has not engaged produce the same automatic responses they always did. The work generalizes only to material that has been directly contacted.

At one year, something structural has shifted. The baseline has stabilized at a higher floor. Returning to the practice after a gap, a week, two weeks, a difficult period, no longer requires rebuilding from the beginning. What was built persists more durably than it did in the early months. The system has, to some degree, internalized the practice.

The presences worked with consistently across that year have changed in their relationship to approach. Not resolved. Still present. Still generating signals. Still in their locations in the space. The stable range for observing them has expanded. What required five sessions to approach carefully a year ago can be approached more directly now. Not because the presences changed. Because the accumulated record of stable contact reduced the defensive charge around them.

What has not changed at one year: the most protected material is still protected. A year builds access to accessible material and starts creating conditions for approaching more defended material. It does not reach the most defended directly. That timeline is longer, and for many people requires clinical support alongside the independent practice.

---

The failure mode at this stage is expecting changes that match the size of the effort.

Months of consistent work producing a reaction that runs forty minutes instead of an hour does not feel proportionate. It produces the conclusion that the system is not working, or that the changes are too small to matter, or that a different approach would work faster.

It will not. Not because this system is the only one that works, but because the changes being produced are the right size for what is being changed. Small shifts in position compound across repeated activations. A reaction that runs slightly shorter each time is a reaction producing slightly less damage across the situations where it appears. A gap that is two seconds wide in one instance and five in another is a gap that is widening. The changes are not dramatic in any single instance because they are not supposed to be. They are cumulative, and their scale is visible only across time.

Measuring against resolution will always disappoint. Resolution is not what is being built. What is being built is a different relationship to what happens. The situations are the same. The signals are the same. What changes is how much of the automatic response you are inside versus how much you can observe.

---

The progress check is three questions, applied after any recent situation involving a familiar signal.

Did the signal register earlier than it would have.

Was the intensity slightly lower, or the duration slightly shorter.

Was there any moment of separation, any point where the signal was visible before the reaction had completed.

If any of these has shifted, even marginally, the system is working.

What changes is not the signal. What changes is where you are standing when it activates, and how much of it arrives with your full unobserved participation versus how much of it you can see while it happens.

That shift, accumulated across time, is what the system produces. Not transformation. Not resolution. A different relationship to what is there. More space. More visibility. More options available in moments that previously had none.

That is what changes.
