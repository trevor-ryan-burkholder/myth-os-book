The system starts as something you do deliberately. Set conditions. Enter the space. Observe. Apply what you have learned in a controlled sequence. Every step is a decision.

That changes through repetition. Not through more effort. Not through better technique. Through the same sequence run enough times that it stops requiring full activation. What was new becomes recognizable. What was recognizable becomes expected. What was expected starts happening before it is called.

---

**The mechanism**

Repetition builds familiarity with specific signals. Their tone. Their speed. The conditions that trigger them. The quality they carry when they arrive. A signal you have encountered fifty times registers differently than one you have encountered twice. The fifty-times signal arrives partially labeled. You do not have to work backward to identify it.

Familiarity also reduces resistance. A signal approached repeatedly in stable conditions, observed without force, engaged without pressure, carries less defensive charge than one that has never been approached directly. The system has recorded that contact does not destabilize anything. Resistance exists to prevent destabilization. When the record shows destabilization has not occurred, resistance decreases. Detection gets easier not because you got better at it. Because the system is no longer actively preventing it.

Easier detection means lower effort. The signal registers before you turn toward it. Recognition arrives with the activation rather than after it. Because less effort is going into detection, more is available for what comes next. The small adjustment. The slight increase in distance. The shift that changes how the signal runs.

---

**The markers**

The markers are not dramatic. They are consistent once you know what to look for.

**Detection gets faster.** A signal that used to take minutes to identify now registers within seconds of activation. You are not ahead of it. You are much less behind it. In a conversation that begins to press on something familiar, you notice the signal arriving rather than noticing, afterward, that it had arrived. The shorter gap is enough to change what is possible.

**Effort drops.** Early on, entering the space required preparation. Quieting the environment. Settling attention. Deliberately lowering the pressure that direct access produces. Over time that preparation shortens. The space is accessible with less setup because the route has been traveled enough times. The same goes for real-time use. The step back happens because it has happened enough times before, not because you decided to initiate it.

**Intensity drops.** Not because the signals change. Not because the presences generating them have been resolved. Because repeated stable contact changes how a signal behaves under contact. A signal approached without force, observed without demand, adjusted without suppression, has a different activation profile than one only ever met with resistance. The peak is lower. The duration is shorter. The aftermath is lighter.

The body marks this too. Early, entering the space required deliberate settling. As the baseline develops, the settling starts happening on its own. The body moves toward the space's register rather than having to be brought there. The physical preparation that took minutes starts taking seconds, then becomes unnecessary. That shift is one of the clearest markers that the practice has moved from deliberate to baseline.

---

**Realistic framing**

None of this is constant. The system does not stay in the same accessible state regardless of circumstances. Under high stress, with insufficient sleep, in situations involving material you have not worked with, detection slows and effort rises.

The baseline is a floor. Not a ceiling. It is the minimum level of awareness available under ordinary conditions, not the maximum available under ideal ones.

Baseline awareness runs quietly in the background under ordinary conditions, becomes less reliable under pressure, and requires deliberate reactivation when something new or more charged is being encountered. That is not a limitation of the system. It is how any integrated skill behaves. The chess player who reads the board automatically in familiar positions still slows down in unfamiliar ones.

---

**In a dissociative system**

Baseline formation in a dissociative system is not one process. It is multiple processes, happening at different rates in different parts, often without coordination between them.

A part that has practiced consistently for months may have real baseline access. The space is familiar. Signals are recognized early. Settling happens quickly. Another part that is rarely present during sessions may have no baseline access at all. The space is unfamiliar. Signals are unrecognized. The practice has to be rebuilt every time that part encounters it.

The baseline you experience is part-specific. The floor is not the same across the whole system. It is the floor available to the part currently present.

Extending the practice to more parts over time happens by creating conditions where less-practiced parts gradually build their own familiarity. Not by forcing participation. Resonance vectors are useful here. A piece of music that produces a consistent response across states can serve as a cross-state anchor that builds familiarity even in parts that are not actively practicing. The somatic grounding moves from the real-time loop transfer similarly. The body responds to them before the part present has cognitive access to the practice.

The other implication: regression in baseline access is more common and less meaningful in a dissociative system than in an integrated one. A day when the floor seems to have dropped may be a day when a less-practiced part is more active. Not a sign the practice has been undone. Holding that distinction prevents discouragement from state-specific variation read as systemic regression.

---

**The failure mode**

The failure mode at this stage is trying to maintain the awareness actively. You notice that detection has become easier. You decide to sustain that by monitoring more carefully. Watching for signals throughout the day. Running the loop in situations that do not require it. Staying alert in a way that looks more like vigilance than awareness.

That effort produces the opposite of what it is trying to preserve. Passive detection works because it is not being driven. Drive it and it requires the same deliberate activation it required at the start.

The marker that the system is becoming baseline is that you notice things without having tried to. If you are trying to notice, the system has not made that move yet. Both are correct positions to be in. The system develops through the trying stage before it reaches the automatic stage. Knowing which stage you are in prevents the error of treating one as the other.

---

Go a day without running the system deliberately. Do not enter the space. Do not look for signals. Do not run the loop. At the end of the day, check whether any of the following occurred without deliberate initiation: a signal registered faster than it would have before, a reaction ran lighter or shorter than expected, you were aware of what was happening mid-situation rather than only in retrospect. If any of those occurred, the system produced them without being asked. That is the marker. If none occurred, the system continues developing through the work already in place.

Either outcome is correct.

What you are looking for is not evidence that the system is working perfectly. You are looking for evidence that it is working without you having to run it.

What required effort begins to happen with less input. That is baseline formation from the inside.
