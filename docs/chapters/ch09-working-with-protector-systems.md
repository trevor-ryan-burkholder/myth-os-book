In a dissociative system, protectors are not subtle.

In an integrated system a protector shows up as a signal that redirects attention, or a quality of resistance at the edge of certain material. You learn to recognize it. Work the edges. Build enough familiarity that it tolerates closer approach over time. The protector is real. It operates inside a system organized around continuity. It can be accustomed to your presence.

In a dissociative system, protectors are often the reason the fragmentation exists. The separation between parts, the walls that keep certain material inaccessible to other parts, is itself a protective function. The system did not fragment randomly. It fragmented because a unified system could not hold the material without coming apart everywhere at once. The protectors are not blocking access to something. In some cases, they are the architecture that made survival possible.

That is a different kind of structure. It requires a different kind of patience than Part 2 prepares you for.

---

**What protector presences look like in a fragmented system**

I have protector presences whose entire function is to keep certain parts of my system out of contact with each other. Not to defend against an outside threat. To maintain the internal separation that keeps the most vulnerable material from flooding the rest of the system at the wrong time.

When I have approached those presences directly, even carefully, even with genuine respect, the response has been immediate and total. Not a gradual drop in clarity. A wall. Session over. Sometimes a switch. Sometimes flooding. Sometimes a blank where the space had been. The protector did not negotiate. It did not warm up. It closed.

What I learned, slowly, through many sessions that ended that way, is that these protectors are not testing whether you are safe enough to let in. They are maintaining a structure that does not operate on that logic. They are not gatekeepers. They are load-bearing walls.

You do not build a relationship with a load-bearing wall by pushing on it. You build it by demonstrating, over a long time, that you are not a structural threat.

---

**Withholding versus destabilizing**

The distinction matters. It is worth being precise about.

A protector that is withholding access produces a specific quality. Clarity drops. The presence becomes less defined or retreats to a less accessible part of the space. Signals quiet or disappear. The body registers closing, tightening, something contracting away from contact. This is the protector doing its job. It is not attacking. It is maintaining separation.

A protector that is destabilizing produces a different quality. Intensity rises instead of dropping. The space becomes less stable rather than more defined. Signals escalate rather than quieting. The body registers alarm rather than withdrawal. Threat instead of retreat. In a dissociative system this can precede a switch, a flood of material from whatever the protector is guarding, or a destabilization that runs well past the session.

The exit criteria for both are the same. Exit immediately.

The implications for the next session differ. A protector that withdrew is telling you where the current edge is. Return further back and try again more slowly. A protector that destabilized is telling you the approach exceeded what the system can hold. Return to basic stability work before going near that area again.

I read the difference in the body before it shows up in the space. Withdrawal contracts. Something pulling back. Destabilization expands. Something pushing outward. The somatic signal is earlier than the cognitive recognition. When the body registers expansion, I exit. Not at the next stable moment. Immediately.

---

**How to approach protector presences in a fragmented system**

The principles from Chapter 5 apply. Observe without forcing. Acknowledge without demanding. Hold position without leaning. What changes is the scale of patience and the additional distance.

**Start further back than feels necessary.** In an integrated system, you can approach to the edge of a presence's stable range and use the boundary as information. In a fragmented system, the boundary is less predictable and the cost of crossing it is more immediate. Start at a distance that produces no response at all. No body change. No shift in the space. That is your baseline. Movement begins from there.

**Make no acknowledgment on first contact.** In Part 2, a single acknowledgment, *I know you're there*, is the permitted opening move. With a strongly defended protector in a fragmented system, acknowledgment itself reads as pressure. The first several sessions near that presence may involve nothing but being in the space while it is present. No acknowledgment. No approach. No attempt at contact. You are demonstrating, through repeated presence, that you nearby does not produce threat. That demonstration takes longer than it feels like it should.

**Acknowledge the protector's function before approaching what it protects.** This is different from acknowledging the presence directly. It is internal recognition, held without being stated, that what the protector is doing is real and has been necessary. Not performance. Actual recognition of the weight of what it is carrying.

I do not say this to the protector. I hold it. There is a difference between performing respect and having it, and protectors in a dissociative system respond to the second and not the first. You cannot think your way to convincing a load-bearing wall. You can be still in its presence long enough that the stillness registers as something other than threat.

---

**What you are not trying to do**

You are not trying to dissolve protectors. You are not trying to bypass them, negotiate with them, or convince them the material they are protecting is now safe to reach. That call is not yours to make. Attempting to make it, even carefully, even with good intentions, is a form of force.

You are also not trying to understand them in the sense of building an explanation for why they exist. The history will surface over time if the relationship develops. Trying to understand it before the relationship is the analytical approach in different clothing. It substitutes explanation for contact. That is the error that produces no movement at the level where movement matters.

What you are trying to do is simpler and slower. Establish your presence as non-threatening across enough repeated encounters that the protector can keep doing its job without treating your observation as an active threat. That is all. The rest follows from that, on the system's timeline, not yours.

---

**Protectors that prevent parts from accessing the practice**

A specific situation that does not come up in integrated work: some protectors in a fragmented system exist to keep certain parts away from internal work. A part may genuinely want to engage and find itself unable to access the space, unable to hold attention through a session, or switching out before anything meaningful occurs. The protector is doing its job. It is preventing access that might expose the material that part is carrying before the system is ready.

The response is not to push through. The response is to work at the level the protector is allowing.

If a part can access the space briefly before the protector closes it, work in that brief window and exit before the close. If a part cannot access the space at all, use resonance vectors as the only available contact with the relevant material. Not as entry points into the space. As the practice itself, for that part, at this stage.

I have parts that have worked with this system for years and still have no reliable access to the full internal space. That is not failure. It is the structure of my system, and the work with those parts happens at the level the structure allows.

The system does not require every part to participate fully. It requires the parts that are participating to do so correctly. What develops from that, across time, is enough.
