The most common expectation at the start of this process is that something will go away. The signals that cause the most difficulty will stop activating. The reactions that create the most problems will be replaced by better ones. The presences that generate the most intensity will be resolved and no longer relevant. That expectation is understandable and it is wrong, and getting clear on what is actually happening, what does and does not change, is necessary for using the system correctly over time.

The signals do not go away. The presences that generate them remain in the space. Difficult situations continue to occur. Reactions still happen, sometimes at full strength, sometimes at the worst possible moment. The system does not remove any of this. What it changes is something narrower and more specific: your position relative to what is happening, and what that position makes available.

---

**Position**

Position is not a metaphor here. It refers to something functional, the degree to which a signal is running you automatically versus the degree to which you have any separation from it. At the start of the process, a signal activates and the response follows immediately and completely. There is no gap. The signal and the reaction are a single event. You are not observing the reaction happening. You are inside it, fully, before awareness has a chance to register what is occurring.

What the system builds, through repeated observation and contact under stable conditions, is a small amount of space between those two things. The signal still activates. The reaction still occurs. But there is a point, brief, partial, sometimes only a few seconds wide, where the activation is visible before the reaction is complete. That point is where something different becomes possible. Not control, not override, not a better response that replaces the automatic one. Only the existence of a gap where previously there was none.

The signal still happens. You are no longer fully inside it.

Somatically, this shift registers as a change in how the body relates to activation. Early in the practice, a signal activates and the body is immediately inside the response: the physical state of the reaction and the reaction itself are identical. As position shifts, the body begins to register the activation as slightly distinct from the state it produces. There is a moment, brief, often only a second or two, where the physical signal is present but the full somatic state of the reaction has not yet arrived. That moment is the gap made physical.

---

**The markers in the short term**

Earlier detection is the first marker. A signal that used to be recognized only in retrospect, after the reaction had already run its full course, begins to register closer to the moment of activation. The reaction may still complete. But the recognition preceded it rather than following it, which changes the information available in the moment. Even a few seconds of additional lead time is a real change in what the moment contains.

Reduced intensity is the second marker. Reactions still occur but feel less overwhelming, not because the signal is producing less charge, but because the relationship to it has shifted. Some of it is being observed rather than experienced, which changes how consuming it feels. The reaction completes, often visibly and with real consequences, but it is slightly less overwhelming than it was.

The third marker is response flexibility. When a gap exists between the signal and the reaction's completion, that gap contains the possibility of a slightly different choice, not a fully considered decision, not a controlled override, but a small variation in how the reaction expresses itself. You still react. You react differently, at least some of the time. The variation is not large. In a situation that would previously have produced a sharp response, what appears instead might be a slightly less sharp one. From the inside, the difference between one available option and two is significant, even when the second option is only marginally better than the first.

---

**What a year looks like**

The short-term markers are visible within weeks to months of consistent practice. The longer arc is different in character, less about individual sessions and more about what has shifted in the underlying structure.

At roughly six months of consistent practice, the most frequently encountered signals are recognizable early enough that real-time use is more reliable. Not consistent, reliable. The gap is there often enough that it is no longer surprising when it appears. Baseline formation has begun in the most-practiced areas of the system. The space is accessible with less preparation. Contact that required careful setup six months ago is available with significantly less overhead.

What has not changed at six months: signals still activate at full strength. The most defended presences are still defended. New situations, ones the practice has not yet encountered, produce the same automatic responses they always did. The work done so far applies to the specific signals and presences that have been worked with. It does not generalize automatically to material that has not been directly engaged.

At roughly one year, something structural has shifted that was not visible earlier. The baseline has stabilized at a genuinely higher floor than at the start. Returning to the practice after a gap, a week, two weeks, a difficult period, no longer requires rebuilding from the beginning. The access that was built persists more durably than it did in the early months, when gaps of a few days were enough to reset significant progress. The system has, to some degree, internalized the practice.

The presences that have been worked with consistently across that year have changed in their relationship to approach. Not resolved, still present, still generating signals, still occupying their locations in the space. But the stable range for observing them has expanded. What required five sessions to approach carefully a year ago can be approached more directly now. Not because they have changed, but because the accumulated record of stable contact has reduced the defensive charge around them.

What has not changed at one year: the most protected material is still protected. Presences associated with the most significant early trauma remain heavily defended in most systems. A year of consistent practice builds access to accessible material and begins to create conditions for approaching more defended material. It does not reach the most defended material directly. That work operates on a longer timeline, and for many people requires clinical support alongside the independent practice.

---

**What becomes permanent and what remains effortful**

Some things that were effortful early in the practice become genuinely automatic with enough time. Signal recognition in familiar situations. The somatic grounding moves of the real-time loop. The basic orientation to the space at the start of a session. These stop requiring deliberate activation. They happen because they have happened enough times.

Some things never become fully automatic because they should not. Approach to heavily defended presences requires deliberate pacing regardless of how much practice has accumulated. The material being approached is still protected, and the protection exists for real reasons. Checking whether a session exceeded the stable range requires deliberate attention, not because the skill has not developed but because the assessment should always be conscious. Exit decisions benefit from remaining deliberate.

The difference between what becomes automatic and what remains effortful is not about skill level. It is about the nature of the task. Routine access to familiar material can be automatic. Contact with protected material should remain deliberate. The deliberateness is not inefficiency, it is the mechanism that makes the contact safe rather than destabilizing.

---

**In a dissociative system**

The long arc in a dissociative system is longer and less linear than in an integrated one. A year of practice in a dissociative system looks different from a year in an integrated one, not because less work has been done, but because the work is distributed across multiple parts with different levels of access and different relationships to the practice.

What a year typically produces in a dissociative system: more parts with some familiarity with the practice. A more robust systemic baseline: the floor is higher even on difficult days. Protectors that previously closed immediately on approach may now allow slightly longer observation windows. The internal communication between parts, where it exists, may be somewhat clearer. Switching that was previously unrelated to sessions may be more connected to recognizable triggers that can be tracked and anticipated.

What a year does not typically produce in a dissociative system: resolution of the deepest fragmentation. Access to the most protected material. A unified experience of the practice across all parts. The timeline for that work is longer, often many years, and it operates at a pace the system sets, not the practitioner.

---

**Calibrating expectations**

The failure mode at this stage is expecting changes that match the size of the effort. Months of consistent work producing a reaction that runs forty minutes instead of an hour does not feel proportionate. It can produce the conclusion that the system is not working, or that the changes are too small to matter, or that a different approach would produce faster results.

Small shifts in position relative to a signal compound across repeated activations. A reaction that runs slightly shorter each time it occurs is a reaction that is producing slightly less damage across the situations where it appears. A gap that is two seconds wide in one instance and five seconds wide in another is a gap that is widening. The changes are not dramatic in any single instance because they are not supposed to be. They are cumulative, and their scale is visible only across time.

Comparing against an expectation of resolution will always produce disappointment, because that is not what is being built. What is being built is a different relationship to what happens. The situations are the same. The signals are the same. What changes is how much of the automatic response you are inside versus how much you can observe.

The progress check is three questions applied after any recent situation that involved a familiar signal. Did the signal register earlier than it would have previously? Was the intensity slightly lower, or the duration slightly shorter? Was there any moment of separation, any point where the signal was visible before the reaction had completed? If any of these has shifted, even marginally, the system is working.

What changes is not the signal. What changes is where you are standing when it activates, and how much of it arrives with your full, unobserved participation versus how much of it you can see while it is happening.

That shift, accumulated across time, is what the system produces. It is not transformation. It is not resolution. It is a different relationship to what is there: more space, more visibility, more options available in the moments that previously had none.

That is what changes. That is what the practice is building toward. Not a new self, but a different position relative to the one already here.
