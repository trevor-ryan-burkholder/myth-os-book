Sessions are controlled. You choose when to enter the space, how long to stay, and when to exit. The conditions are set in advance. The only variables are the ones you introduce. That control is what makes sessions useful for building the system, and it is also what makes them different from everything else.

Real situations are not controlled. They arrive without notice, run on their own timeline, and do not pause while you orient yourself. A conversation escalates before you have registered that something is happening. A reaction is already underway before you have identified the signal. By the time you notice what is occurring, you are already inside it. This is not a failure of the system. It is the condition the system has to work in.

---

**The standard**

The question is not whether real-time use will be clean. It will not be. The question is whether partial, imperfect application produces anything useful, and it does, consistently, once you stop measuring it against what sessions produce.

In a session, you can observe a presence from a stable position, make light contact, and exit cleanly. In a real situation, you will often notice a signal only after it has already begun moving you. You will catch it mid-reaction, or recognize it only when the intensity starts to drop. That recognition still counts. Noticing what happened ten seconds ago is not the same as noticing in advance, but it is not useless. It gives you a reference point for the next time, and it begins to shorten the gap between activation and awareness. The gap closes through repetition, the same mechanism that builds access in sessions.

Partial improvement is the standard. Not elimination, reduction. Even a small reduction in intensity is a real change, because intensity is what drives the outcomes you are trying to shift. A reaction at seventy percent force produces different results than one at full force, even if both are still reactions. The goal is not to stop reacting. It is to introduce a small amount of space between the signal and its full expression.

---

**The loop**

The shorthand for real-time use is: **notice → step back → adjust.**

Notice means recognizing that a familiar signal has activated. Not analyzing it, not identifying which presence it points to, not running any part of the system in detail. Only the recognition: *this is that signal.* The familiarity is the marker, the same tone, the same speed, the same quality of response you have encountered before. That recognition is enough to work with.

The somatic channel is often faster than the cognitive one here. Before you have consciously registered that a signal has activated, the body has already responded, a tightening in a specific location, a change in breathing quality, a quality of forward pressure or withdrawal that is characteristic of this particular signal. Learning to read the body's early signal gives you more time to work with than waiting for cognitive recognition. The notice step, used well, is often a somatic notice first.

Step back means creating a slight internal distance from the signal. Not disengaging from the situation, not suppressing the response, not trying to make it stop. A small internal movement, the same distance adjustment practiced in the space, applied in compressed form. The signal is still active. You are still in the situation. You have shifted your position relative to the signal by a small amount, which changes how much of it is running you at full strength.

The somatic version of stepping back has a specific quality. It is not withdrawal from the situation, it is a slight internal settling, a quality of the body becoming less fused with the signal's momentum. It can be as small as a single exhale that is slightly longer than the previous one, or a momentary attending to physical ground, feet on the floor, weight in the chair. Something that registers the body as distinct from the signal rather than identical with it.

Adjust means noticing whether that shift changed anything. Not looking for a dramatic change. Not expecting the reaction to disappear. Observing whether intensity is slightly lower, whether there is slightly more room to move, whether the situation feels marginally more manageable than it did two seconds ago. If it does, that is the result. If it does not, the situation continues regardless, and you have lost nothing by trying.

---

**What it looks like**

You are in a conversation and something the other person says produces an immediate tightening in the chest, a somatic signal you recognize from sessions, from previous situations with a similar quality. You are already responding before you have caught it consciously. But the body registered it first, and that registration gives you two seconds you would not otherwise have had. In those two seconds you create a small amount of internal distance. The response continues, but slightly less at full force. You say something you might still regret, but not the version you would have said without the adjustment. That is a valid result.

Or you are in a situation that is escalating and you notice a familiar quality of rising intensity, same rate, same physical location as signals you have tracked before. You do not stop the escalation. You exhale slightly, notice the feet on the floor, step back internally by a small amount and observe it rather than being fully inside it. The escalation may continue. But you have more information about what is happening, which changes how you move through it. That is a valid result.

Or you are already deep in a reaction, minutes in, not seconds, and you recognize what has been running. The recognition comes late. The signal has already produced most of its outcome. But you name it internally: *this is that signal.* The naming does not undo what happened. It shortens the time spent inside the aftermath, and it adds another data point that makes earlier recognition more likely next time. That is also a valid result.

---

**Real-time use in a dissociative system**

If you have a dissociative system, real-time use has an additional variable: you may not be the same part in the middle of a difficult situation that you were when you developed familiarity with a particular signal.

A signal that is well-recognized by one part may be entirely unfamiliar to another. A reaction that the practicing part can catch early may run completely unrecognized by a different part who is now running things because the situation triggered a switch. This is not a failure of the practice. It is a feature of a dissociative system under pressure.

The practical adjustment: in a dissociative system, the notice step adds a second question to *this is that signal*. The second question is *which part am I right now, and does this part have access to the practice?* That second question does not need to be answered analytically. It is more of an orienting check, a brief attending to whether the current state has familiarity with internal work, or whether it is operating without that reference.

When a part is present that does not have access to the practice, the loop is not available in its full form. What is available is the somatic step only, a single grounding move, the exhale, the physical contact with ground, without the cognitive recognition layer. That is enough to introduce a small amount of space even when the full loop is not available. It is not optimal. It is better than nothing, and it is what is available in that state.

Developing the practice across multiple parts, so that more of the system has access to at least the somatic component of the loop, is a longer-term goal, not an immediate one. It happens through the same mechanism as everything else in this system: repeated stable contact, over time, building familiarity in parts that did not previously have it.

---

**The common failures**

The most common failure in real-time use is expecting it to work like a session. You notice a signal and try to run the full process, locate the space, find the presence, establish distance, maintain stability. That sequence requires conditions that do not exist in real situations. Attempting it mid-conversation produces overthinking, which adds cognitive load at exactly the moment the system is already under pressure. The signal intensifies, the situation moves forward without you, and you end up more reactive than if you had done nothing.

Less, not more. Notice the signal, somatically if possible, cognitively if not. Step back by the smallest usable amount. Observe what changes. Continue. Three moves, compressed, applied once. The system is not being run in real time, it is being referenced. The sessions are the training. Real time is the application of what the training made available.

Expecting full control is the other failure. A signal that has been activating for years will not be overridden by months of session work and a moment of real-time awareness. What is happening is that the activation runs slightly differently than it would have before, slightly less automatic, slightly more visible, with slightly more room between the signal and the response. That difference accumulates. It is not visible in any single instance. Across repeated situations it produces real change in how the signals behave and what they produce.

The measure is not perfection. It is whether the gap between activation and awareness shortened, and whether the response produced slightly less damage than it would have without the adjustment. By that measure, imperfect use is still use, and late recognition is still recognition.
