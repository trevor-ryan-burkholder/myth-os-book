Resonance points toward something. This chapter is about what it points toward.

The mind has a geography. Not as metaphor. As a functional description of how attention behaves when it stops trying to think and starts trying to perceive. There are locations. There are distances. Areas feel qualitatively different from each other. You will not see this until you stop looking for a process and start looking for a place.

Two sessions follow. Do not run them back to back. Do the first. Let it settle. Return for the second when the first feels finished.

---

**You are not building anything**

The imagination has two modes. Construction generates what you ask it to generate. Perception receives what is already there. They feel different. Constructed material has a quality of deliberateness. Perceived material arrives on its own, often vague, often partial, with the feel of noticing rather than making.

If what shows up feels invented, you have shifted into construction. Reduce effort. Wait. Construction tells you what you expected. Perception tells you what is there. Only one of those is useful.

The same rule covers the body. You are not producing sensations. You are noticing what the body is already doing as attention moves toward the internal space. Some people read imagery first. Others read the body first. Both channels are real and both carry information. Use whichever arrives more clearly.

---

**Session One: Detection**

Sit somewhere quiet. Close your eyes. Let attention settle. When it has steadied, ask one question internally, once:

*If my mind were a place, what is already there?*

Then stop. Do not repeat it. Do not answer it analytically. Drop it the way you would drop a stone into water and watch the surface.

Look for properties, not detail. Is there a sense of space at all. Open or enclosed. Light, dark, or something in between. Is there a tone, a quality, a sense that something is present.

Notice the body in parallel. A settling or a tightening. Warmth or coolness in a specific location. Weight or lightness. The body is reading the same territory the imagery points toward.

Results vary. Some people get a clear sense of dimension on the first attempt. Some get darkness with texture. Some get fragments that appear and fade. Some notice the body before any imagery, a settling in the chest, attention shifting downward. Some notice nothing. Absence of clear imagery is not failure. It is information about current conditions.

Hold the session to about ten minutes. Do not extend it. Do not try to sharpen what you see or fill in what is missing. When the time is up, open your eyes, return to the room, and let the experience go.

The common failure here is construction. Forcing imagery. Filling in what is not arriving on its own. If you catch yourself doing it, reduce effort and observe what remains.

You have made first contact with the environment, not with anything inside it. Vague is fine. Incomplete is fine. Something is there.

---

**Session Two: Structure**

Detection produces something loose. Areas bleeding into each other. No sense of near or far. No way to regulate exposure. The second session adds enough organization to operate.

Structure is not detail. You do not need a rendered environment. You need three things: zones, boundaries, distance.

Zones are areas that feel qualitatively different. One area feels stable. Another feels charged or unclear. A third feels empty. These are not categories you impose. They are differences you notice. The stable area is stable because that is how it presents, not because you decided.

Boundaries follow from zones. The moment two areas feel different, a boundary already exists between them. Naming it does one thing: it keeps the unstable area from leaking into the stable one.

Distance is the primary control lever. The closer something is, the more strongly it registers. This is not metaphorical. A signal that feels overwhelming at close range becomes manageable from farther away. You have not changed what is there. You have changed your position relative to it.

Return to the environment from the first session. Let it appear as it did. Then ask three questions in sequence: Where does the space feel most stable. Where does it feel charged or unclear. What feels closest right now.

Notice the body alongside. Where does the stable area register physically. Is there a tightening, a shift in breath, a change in where weight sits near the charged areas. The body's read of zones is as real as the imagery and sometimes more informative.

If anything feels too close, shift it slightly farther away. Notice what changes in both the imagery and the body. That is the only adjustment this session requires. Do not expand the environment. Do not add structure that is not already implied.

The failure mode is construction again. A space that behaves according to your expectations tells you nothing about what is there.

---

Structure does not give you clarity. It gives you the ability to stay in the space without being pulled into it. You can hold a position. Regulate distance. Return to stable ground when something escalates.

You now have a channel for perception and an environment stable enough to operate in.
