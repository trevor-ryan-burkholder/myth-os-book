Everything in Part 2 assumes a degree of continuity. You enter the space, observe what is present, make contact, exit, and return next session to find something recognizable from before. The space persists. The presences in it persist. The work you did yesterday is available today. Memory connects the sessions into a coherent practice.

For some people, that continuity does not hold. Not because the practice is failing, but because the system they are working with is not organized around continuity. It is organized around separation.

This section is for those people.

---

**What a fragmented system looks like**

I have Dissociative Identity Disorder. That means my internal system is not a single unified structure, it is a collection of distinct parts, each carrying different material, different memories, different ways of operating. Some of those parts are aware of each other. Some are not. Some have access to what happened yesterday. Others do not. What I experience as the internal space in one state is not always the same space I encounter in another.

This is not metaphorical fragmentation, the vague sense that different versions of yourself show up in different contexts. It is structural. There are parts of my system that genuinely do not share information with other parts. Sessions done in one state may not be accessible to the part doing the next session. A presence approached carefully over weeks may be unknown to the part that shows up on a difficult day.

If this is your situation, you already know it. You may not have the clinical label. You may be working with OSDD rather than DID, or with severe dissociative responses to trauma that fall short of a formal diagnosis. The specifics matter less than the functional reality: your internal system does not operate as a unified whole, and the practice described in Part 2 needs to be held differently to work within that structure.

If this is not your situation, if your sense of internal fragmentation is more metaphorical than structural, Part 2 is sufficient. This section will still be informative, but the specific constraints it describes are not constraints you are working within.

---

**What changes and what does not**

The principles of Part 2 do not change for a dissociative system. Indirect access, stability before depth, minimal input, no forcing, these remain the operating principles and in fact become more important, not less. The constraints exist for a reason. In a dissociative system the consequences of violating them are faster and more complete. The system can switch states, fragment further, or produce flooding that is harder to contain when the underlying structure is already separated.

What changes is the relationship between sessions. In an integrated system, sessions build on each other continuously. In a dissociative system, they may not. Different parts may conduct different sessions. The part present today may not have access to what the part present last week encountered. This is not failure. It is the structure of the system, and the practice needs to account for it.

The practical implications:

**Each session may need to begin from scratch.** Not because previous sessions did not happen, but because the part present may not have access to them. This means the opening moves of each session, detecting the space, locating the structure, checking what is present, are not formalities to rush through. They are genuine first contact with conditions that may have changed since the last session, conducted by a part that may be encountering them for the first time.

**The space may look different depending on who is in it.** My internal space does not appear the same in every state. Some parts access it as a structured environment with familiar zones and recognizable presences. Others encounter it as darker, more chaotic, less navigable. Others seem to have no access at all: the space is simply not available in that state. This is normal for a dissociative system and does not indicate that the work is not working. It indicates which parts have developed familiarity with the space and which have not.

**Presences may be unknown to the part currently working.** In an integrated system, a presence encountered over multiple sessions becomes familiar: you know its location, its character, its approximate function. In a dissociative system, a part conducting a session may encounter presences that other parts have worked with extensively but that this part has never seen before. The approach principles still apply: observe without forcing, acknowledge without demanding. But the accumulated familiarity from previous sessions may not be available to this part. Treat it as first contact.

**Continuity across states**

The absence of automatic continuity does not mean continuity is impossible. It means it requires deliberate cultivation rather than being assumed.

A few things that help:

Keep brief external records of sessions. Not detailed analysis. That pulls the work back into the analytical layer. Brief notes: what part was present, what area of the space was accessible, whether anything was encountered, what the body registered. These notes are available across states in a way that internal memory may not be. Reading them at the start of a session, briefly, without over-relying on them, can provide a thread of continuity even when internal memory does not.

I keep a simple log. Date, which part, what the space felt like, whether anything was present. I do not try to create a narrative from it. I use it the way you would use a map that other people have drawn, as orientation, not instruction.

Resonance vectors can function as cross-state anchors. A piece of music that produces a strong response in one state often produces a response in other states, even when those states have no shared memory of having used it. The resonance is not stored in episodic memory, it is stored at a level the dissociative system does not fully separate. Using a resonance vector at the start of a session can help orient toward the space even when the part present has no direct access to previous sessions.

Not all parts will engage with the system. Some parts of a dissociative system are not interested in this kind of work, do not have access to the internal space, or are actively resistant to internal work. This is not something to force. The system described in this book works with willing parts. A part that is not willing or not capable of this work at this time is not a problem to be solved. It is a condition to be respected.

---

**The additional safety requirement**

In an integrated system, the stability principles function as good practice. In a dissociative system, they function as structural requirements. The difference matters.

When I push past the stable range in a session, when I stay too long, push for more clarity than the system is offering, or try to force contact with a presence that is not ready, the consequence is not only a harder next session. It can be a switch. A shift to a part that is less stable, less equipped to handle what was encountered, or carrying material that was activated by the overreach. The flooding that occurs in an integrated system when stability is exceeded occurs faster and more completely in a dissociative one, because the parts holding vulnerable material are structurally closer to the surface.

This means the exit criteria in Chapter 7 are not guidelines, they are stops. Rising intensity: exit. Clarity dropping: exit. Body signaling escalating pressure: exit. The urge to push further arrives earlier in a dissociative system and is less reliable as a signal of genuine opportunity. Treat it as a warning regardless.

It also means that the period after a session requires attention. Switching after a session is a signal that the session exceeded what the current part could hold. If you notice a state change in the hours following a session, a shift in how you feel, a quality of dislocation, a sense that a different part is now running things, that is information about where the session's limits actually were. Note it and reduce the intensity of the next session accordingly.

---

**What this section is for**

The next two chapters address protector systems in a fragmented context and what baseline stability means when the system has multiple discrete states. Both of those topics require the foundation laid here: that the fragmentation is structural, that the principles of Part 2 still apply but with less margin, and that working within this structure requires patience at a different scale than working within an integrated one.

The system described in this book was built inside a dissociative system. The conservatism of its constraints, the emphasis on stability over depth, the insistence on minimal input, the repeated instruction to exit before the boundary rather than after it, come directly from the cost of not observing them. Not in theory. In practice, in sessions that went wrong, in switches and flooding and the work of stabilizing afterward.

The constraints are not overcautious. They are calibrated to the actual conditions of this kind of work. If your system is fragmented, they are calibrated to yours.
