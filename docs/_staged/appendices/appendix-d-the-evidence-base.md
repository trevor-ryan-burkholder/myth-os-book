This appendix is for readers who want to know where the ideas in this book come from and what evidentiary status they carry. It is worth being direct about both.

Myth-OS is not a validated clinical modality. No randomized controlled trials have been run on this specific approach. No outcome studies exist. The system has been tested extensively in one person's practice across years of working with a dissociative system and complex trauma, and that is a meaningful form of evidence, but it is not the kind that clinical psychology requires before making efficacy claims. This book does not make efficacy claims. It describes a practice and its mechanisms.

What the system does have is a theoretical basis that is grounded in established research traditions, and a set of mechanisms that are consistent with, though not derived from, a substantial body of existing work. The distinction between "consistent with" and "derived from" matters. The system did not emerge from a literature review. It emerged from practice necessity. The literature provides post-hoc theoretical support for why it works, not the basis on which it was built.

What follows is an honest account of the research each component of the system draws on, what that research actually supports, and where the extrapolation begins.

---

**Internal Family Systems**

The multiplicity premise, that the psyche naturally organizes into distinct functional parts rather than operating as a unified agent, is the foundation of the signal/presence distinction that runs throughout this book. This premise is most thoroughly developed in Internal Family Systems therapy, created by Richard Schwartz (Schwartz, 1995; Schwartz & Sweezy, 2019).

IFS has a growing evidence base. A randomized controlled trial published in the Journal of Rheumatology (Shadick et al., 2013) demonstrated significant improvements in rheumatoid arthritis patients receiving IFS therapy compared to controls, including reductions in pain, depression, and self-compassion improvements. A pilot study in the Journal of Psychotherapy Integration (Laithwaite et al., 2009) showed positive outcomes for IFS with psychosis populations. A 2021 study in the Journal of Aggression, Maltreatment & Trauma (Anderson et al., 2021) found IFS effective for PTSD symptom reduction. The American Psychological Association has listed IFS as an evidence-based practice for PTSD as of 2015.

This system draws on IFS in two specific ways. First, the functional premise: presences exist and can be observed independently of whether they are recognized, and addressing signals without reaching the presences generating them produces only temporary change. Second, the approach principle: you do not force parts to change. You build a relationship with them through repeated stable contact. Both of these are IFS principles applied through a different access mechanism than IFS itself uses.

Where this system diverges from IFS: IFS has a developed therapeutic protocol including guided unblending, direct access to Self energy, and a specific unburdening process for exiles. This system includes none of that. It is more conservative in what it attempts and more applicable outside a clinical relationship as a result. It builds the conditions for IFS work rather than replicating it.

**Somatic Approaches and Trauma**

The somatic layer integrated throughout this book draws primarily on Bessel van der Kolk's work on trauma and the body (van der Kolk, 2014) and Peter Levine's somatic experiencing framework (Levine, 1997).

Van der Kolk's research demonstrates that trauma is stored somatically, that the body carries the imprint of traumatic experience in ways that are not accessible through cognitive processing alone, and that effective trauma treatment must engage the body rather than bypassing it. This is the theoretical basis for treating somatic signals as co-equal with visual/spatial perception throughout the detection and approach work in this book. The body is not a secondary feedback channel, it is often the primary and faster channel.

Levine's somatic experiencing work provides the specific observation that the body registers activation and deactivation in ways that precede cognitive awareness, and that working with the body's own completion tendencies, rather than forcing cognitive processing of traumatic material, produces more durable nervous system regulation. This is consistent with the approach principles throughout: the somatic signal of escalating pressure arrives before the cognitive recognition of it, and attending to that signal is what makes early exit possible.

The evidence base for somatic approaches to trauma is substantial. A meta-analysis by Kuhfuß et al. (2021) in the European Journal of Psychotraumatology found significant effects for somatic therapy approaches across trauma populations. EMDR, which has strong evidence support (Shapiro, 2018), incorporates somatic tracking as a central component.

This system extrapolates from this research in one specific direction: it treats the somatic channel as a real-time indicator of internal state during sessions, equivalent in reliability to visual/spatial perception of the internal environment. That specific application has not been studied directly. It is consistent with the underlying research on somatic awareness and trauma, but it is an extrapolation rather than a finding.

**Jungian Psychology and Symbolic Access**

The resonance mechanism, the observation that certain symbols produce pre-analytical responses that bypass cognitive resistance and connect directly to protected material, draws on Jung's analytical psychology (Jung, 1969) and the broader tradition of archetypal psychology.

Jung's core empirical observation, made across decades of clinical practice and cross-cultural study, was that the psyche organizes experience through symbolic and narrative structures that predate language and operate independently of conscious cognition. The specific finding most relevant here is that symbolic material, mythological figures, images, narrative structures, produces responses in the psyche that direct cognitive engagement does not, and that these responses are not random but consistent with the individual's specific psychological organization.

The evidence base for Jungian approaches is more contested than for IFS or somatic work. Jungian analysis is difficult to study with standard RCT methodology because it is long-term, highly individualized, and does not lend itself to manualization. What evidence exists is largely case-based and qualitative. Several studies have examined the efficacy of expressive arts therapies that draw on Jungian principles (Slayton et al., 2010), with generally positive findings for trauma and depression populations, though methodological limitations are significant.

The resonance mechanism this system uses is more modest than Jungian analysis in its claims. It does not require the reader to accept the existence of archetypes, the collective unconscious, or any theoretical apparatus from analytical psychology. It requires only the empirically observable fact, verifiable in direct experience, that some symbols produce immediate, strong responses that others do not, and that those responses are consistent over time. The Jungian framework provides a theoretical account of why this happens. The system does not depend on that account being correct. It depends only on the phenomenon being real, which it is for anyone who has experienced it.

**Narrative Therapy**

The emphasis on observation over interpretation, on allowing meaning to emerge from accumulated data rather than assigning it, draws on the narrative therapy tradition developed by Michael White and David Epston (White & Epston, 1990).

Narrative therapy's core insight is that premature narrative closure, assigning a fixed meaning to experience before it has been fully observed, reduces the complexity and accuracy of the understanding produced. This is the theoretical basis for the repeated instruction throughout this book not to name presences or assign functions before enough observation has accumulated. A label, once applied, becomes a lens that filters subsequent observation. The data that does not fit gets discarded rather than integrated.

The evidence base for narrative therapy is moderate. A systematic review by Etchison and Kleist (2000) found consistent positive outcomes across studies, and narrative approaches have demonstrated efficacy specifically for trauma processing and identity-related difficulties. A more recent review by Vromans and Schweitzer (2011) found significant pre-post effects for depression in narrative therapy.

This system draws on narrative therapy's anti-interpretive stance rather than its full therapeutic protocol, which involves externalization, unique outcomes, and re-authoring conversations conducted in a therapeutic relationship. The principle that meaning should emerge rather than be imposed is applied here to internal observation rather than to life narrative construction.

**Dual-Process Theory and Indirect Access**

The foundational claim of this book, that direct cognitive effort cannot reach the material generating automatic responses, because those two things operate on different levels, is consistent with dual-process theory in cognitive psychology (Kahneman, 2011; Evans, 2008).

Dual-process theory distinguishes between System 1 processes (fast, automatic, associative, emotionally driven) and System 2 processes (slow, deliberate, analytical, effortful). Trauma-based responses, early-formed protective responses, and the defensive structures that prevent access to protected material all operate primarily in System 1. Analytical effort, insight, and deliberate introspection operate primarily in System 2. The two systems interact, but System 2 cannot simply override System 1 through direct effort, which is precisely what the access problem described in Chapter 1 amounts to.

The indirect access approach this system uses, resonance, symbolic engagement, somatic tracking, operates in a register closer to System 1 than direct cognitive effort does. This is not a claim that Myth-OS is a dual-process intervention. It is an observation that the mechanism by which indirect access works is consistent with what dual-process theory predicts about what can and cannot be reached through direct cognitive effort.

Kahneman's Thinking, Fast and Slow (2011) provides the most accessible account of dual-process theory for general readers. The research base underlying it is extensive and well-established.

**Summary of evidentiary status**

The components of this system and their evidentiary status:

The multiplicity premise (the psyche organizes into functional parts): **well-supported** by IFS research and consistent with dissociation research more broadly.

The somatic tracking approach (the body as co-equal feedback channel): **well-supported** by trauma research and somatic therapy outcome studies.

The indirect access principle (symbolic/resonance engagement reaches material that direct effort cannot): **theoretically supported** by dual-process theory and consistent with Jungian clinical observation, but not directly studied in this specific form.

The approach and contact mechanics (stability before depth, minimal input, no forcing): **consistent with** IFS approach principles and trauma-informed practice principles, but not studied independently.

This specific system as a whole: **not studied**. No outcome trials exist. The evidence is the accumulated practice of one person across years, which is the form of evidence appropriate to a field guide rather than a clinical manual.

This book is a field guide. It describes what to do and how to do it, based on what has worked, grounded in frameworks that have theoretical and empirical support. It does not claim more than that. Readers who want to explore the underlying research traditions in depth should begin with the primary sources listed below.
