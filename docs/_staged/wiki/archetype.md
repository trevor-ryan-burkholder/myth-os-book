A term from Jungian psychology. A structural tendency in the human psyche to organize experience in certain recurring ways: the Hero, the [Shadow]({{ '/wiki/shadow/' | relative_url }}), the Mother, the Trickster, and others.

An archetype is not a specific image or figure. It is the underlying pattern that different cultures and individuals express through different symbols. The Hero is not a specific character from a specific mythology. It is a pattern of meaning-making that appears wherever humans face the challenge of confronting the unknown, overcoming adversity, and returning changed.

What actually appears in any given person's psychology, the specific images, figures, and symbols through which these structural tendencies manifest, is shaped by culture, personal history, and individual experience. Two people can carry the same archetypal material and express it through entirely different symbols. What they share is the underlying structure, not the surface form.

[Resonance vectors]({{ '/wiki/resonance-vector/' | relative_url }}) connect to archetypal structures, not to the symbols themselves. This is why a symbol that produces strong [Resonance]({{ '/wiki/resonance/' | relative_url }}) in one person does not produce the same response in another, and why the responses are not random. When something produces a strong immediate response, a character, a piece of music, an image, it is because the symbol has connected with an underlying structural tendency in the psyche. The symbol is not the archetype. It is the form through which the archetype is currently expressing itself.

Myth-OS does not require accepting Jung's full theoretical framework. The system works whether or not archetypes exist as Jung described them. The Jungian account provides the most developed existing explanation of why [Resonance]({{ '/wiki/resonance/' | relative_url }}) works as a mechanism, but the practice depends only on the empirically observable fact that resonance responses occur and are consistent over time.
