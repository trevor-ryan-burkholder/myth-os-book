What a signal consistently does. The outcome it produces when it activates, as distinct from why it exists or what it means.

Tracking function rather than cause keeps observation at the level of what is actually happening rather than at the level of explanation. A signal that shuts down engagement whenever emotional exposure increases is performing a function: preventing further exposure. Understanding the function is more useful than explaining the cause, because function is observable in real time and explanation is inferred after the fact.

Asking why a signal exists pulls attention toward history, toward the conditions that formed the response, toward understanding that feels complete but changes nothing about how the signal behaves. Asking what a signal does pulls attention toward observation, toward the outcome it produces in real time, which is the only thing you can track without inference.

The practical move is two questions in sequence. First: what does this do, and what outcome does it produce when it activates? Second: what would happen if it did not activate? The second question often reveals the function more clearly than the first because it shows what the signal is preventing rather than what it is producing.
