This appendix is for readers who want to know where the ideas in this book come from and what evidentiary weight they carry. Worth being direct about both.

Myth-OS is not a validated clinical modality. No RCTs have been run on this specific approach. No outcome studies exist. The system has been tested in one person's practice across years of working with a dissociative system and complex trauma, which is a meaningful form of evidence, but not the kind clinical psychology requires before making efficacy claims. This book does not make efficacy claims. It describes a practice and its mechanisms.

What the system has is a theoretical basis grounded in established research traditions and a set of mechanisms consistent with, though not derived from, a substantial body of existing work. The distinction matters. The system did not emerge from a literature review. It emerged from practice necessity. The literature provides post-hoc support, not the basis on which it was built.

What follows is an honest account of what each component draws on, what the research supports, and where the extrapolation begins.

---

**Internal Family Systems**

The multiplicity premise, that the psyche organizes into distinct functional parts rather than operating as a unified agent, is the foundation of the signal-and-presence distinction throughout this book. The most developed account is Internal Family Systems (Schwartz, 1995; Schwartz & Sweezy, 2019).

IFS has a growing evidence base. An RCT in the Journal of Rheumatology (Shadick et al., 2013) showed significant improvements in rheumatoid arthritis patients receiving IFS therapy compared to controls, including reductions in pain and depression. A pilot study in the Journal of Psychotherapy Integration (Laithwaite et al., 2009) showed positive outcomes for IFS with a psychosis population. A 2021 study in the Journal of Aggression, Maltreatment & Trauma (Anderson et al., 2021) found IFS effective for PTSD symptom reduction. The American Psychological Association listed IFS as an evidence-based practice for PTSD in 2015.

This system draws on IFS in two specific ways. First, the functional premise: [presences](/wiki/presence/) exist and can be observed independently of whether they are recognized, and addressing [signals](/wiki/signal/) without reaching the presences generating them produces only temporary change. Second, the approach principle: you do not force parts to change. You build a relationship through repeated stable contact.

Where this system diverges: IFS has a developed therapeutic protocol including guided unblending, direct Self access, and an unburdening process for exiles. This system includes none of that. It is more conservative, and works outside a clinical relationship as a result. It builds the conditions for IFS-style work rather than replicating it.

**Somatic approaches and trauma**

The somatic layer integrated throughout this book draws primarily on Bessel van der Kolk's work on trauma and the body (van der Kolk, 2014) and Peter Levine's somatic experiencing framework (Levine, 1997).

Van der Kolk's research demonstrates that trauma is stored somatically, that the body carries traumatic imprint in ways cognitive processing alone cannot reach, and that effective trauma treatment must engage the body rather than bypass it. That is the basis for treating somatic signals as co-equal with visual-spatial perception throughout this book. The body is not a secondary channel. It is often the primary and faster one.

Levine's somatic experiencing work provides the specific observation that the body registers activation and deactivation before cognition catches up, and that working with the body's own completion tendencies produces more durable nervous system regulation. This aligns with the approach principles here: the somatic signal of escalating pressure arrives before the cognitive recognition of it, and attending to it is what makes early exit possible.

The evidence base is substantial. A meta-analysis by Kuhfuß et al. (2021) in the European Journal of Psychotraumatology found significant effects for somatic therapy approaches across trauma populations. EMDR, with strong evidence support (Shapiro, 2018), incorporates somatic tracking as a central component.

This system extrapolates in one specific direction: treating the somatic channel as a real-time indicator of internal state during sessions, equivalent in reliability to visual-spatial perception of the internal environment. That specific application has not been studied directly. It is consistent with the underlying research, but extrapolation, not finding.

**Jungian psychology and symbolic access**

The [resonance](/wiki/resonance/) mechanism, the observation that certain symbols produce pre-analytical responses that bypass cognitive resistance, draws on Jung's analytical psychology (Jung, 1969) and the broader archetypal tradition.

Jung's core empirical observation, made across decades of practice and cross-cultural study, was that the psyche organizes experience through symbolic and narrative structures that predate language and operate independently of conscious cognition. Symbolic material, mythological figures, images, narrative structures, produces responses in the psyche that direct cognitive engagement does not, and those responses are not random.

The evidence base for Jungian approaches is more contested than for IFS or somatic work. Jungian analysis is hard to study with standard RCT methodology because it is long-term, individualized, and resists manualization. What evidence exists is largely case-based and qualitative. Studies of expressive arts therapies drawing on Jungian principles (Slayton et al., 2010) report generally positive findings for trauma and depression, though methodological limits are significant.

The resonance mechanism this system uses is more modest than Jungian analysis in its claims. It does not require accepting archetypes, the collective unconscious, or any theoretical apparatus. It requires only the empirically observable fact, verifiable in direct experience, that some symbols produce immediate strong responses and others do not, consistently over time. The Jungian framework explains why. The system does not depend on the explanation being correct. It depends on the phenomenon being real.

**Narrative therapy**

The emphasis on observation over interpretation, on letting meaning emerge from accumulated data rather than imposing it, draws on the narrative therapy tradition developed by Michael White and David Epston (White & Epston, 1990).

Narrative therapy's core insight is that premature narrative closure, assigning fixed meaning before observation is complete, reduces the complexity and accuracy of the understanding produced. That is the basis for the repeated instruction in this book not to name presences or assign functions before enough observation has accumulated. A label, once applied, becomes a lens. Data that does not fit gets discarded rather than integrated.

The evidence base for narrative therapy is moderate. A systematic review by Etchison and Kleist (2000) reported consistent positive outcomes across studies, with documented efficacy for trauma processing and identity-related difficulties. A later review by Vromans and Schweitzer (2011) reported significant pre-post effects for depression.

This system draws on narrative therapy's anti-interpretive stance rather than its full protocol, which involves externalization, unique outcomes, and re-authoring in a therapeutic relationship. The principle that meaning should emerge rather than be imposed is applied here to internal observation, not to life-narrative construction.

---

**Dual-process theory and indirect access**

The foundational claim of this book, that direct cognitive effort cannot reach the material generating automatic responses because the two operate on different levels, is consistent with dual-process theory in cognitive psychology (Kahneman, 2011; Evans, 2008).

Dual-process theory distinguishes System 1 processes (fast, automatic, associative, emotionally driven) from System 2 processes (slow, deliberate, analytical, effortful). Trauma-based responses, early protective responses, and the defensive structures preventing access to protected material all operate primarily in System 1. Analytical effort and deliberate introspection operate primarily in System 2. The two interact, but System 2 cannot override System 1 through direct effort, which is the access problem described in Chapter 1.

The indirect access approach this system uses, resonance, symbolic engagement, somatic tracking, operates closer to System 1 than direct cognitive effort does. This is not a claim that Myth-OS is a dual-process intervention. It is an observation that the mechanism by which indirect access works is consistent with what dual-process theory predicts about what direct cognitive effort can and cannot reach.

Kahneman's *Thinking, Fast and Slow* (2011) is the accessible account. The underlying research base is extensive and well-established.

---

**Summary of evidentiary status**

The multiplicity premise (the psyche organizes into functional parts): **well-supported** by IFS research and consistent with dissociation research more broadly.

The somatic tracking approach (the body as co-equal feedback channel): **well-supported** by trauma research and somatic therapy outcome studies.

The indirect access principle (symbolic/resonance engagement reaches material direct effort cannot): **theoretically supported** by dual-process theory and consistent with Jungian clinical observation, not directly studied in this specific form.

The approach and contact mechanics (stability before depth, minimal input, no forcing): **consistent with** IFS approach principles and trauma-informed practice, not studied independently.

This specific system as a whole: **not studied**. No outcome trials. The evidence is the accumulated practice of one person across years, which is the form of evidence appropriate to a field guide, not a clinical manual.

This book is a field guide. It describes what to do and how to do it, grounded in frameworks with theoretical and empirical support. It claims nothing more. Readers who want to dig into the underlying research should start with the primary sources below.
