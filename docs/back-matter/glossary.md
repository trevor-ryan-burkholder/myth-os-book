---

**Access**
The ability to make contact with internal material, signals, presences, and the space, in a way that is stable and workable. Access is not the same as understanding. You can understand something without having access to what is generating it. Access is built through repetition and indirect approach, not through force or insight.

---

**Acknowledgment**
The only permitted form of direct input during contact with a presence. A single internal statement, *I know you're there*, said once and not followed up. An acknowledgment carries no demand for a response. It is a statement of awareness, not the opening of a dialogue. Anything beyond a single acknowledgment introduces pressure.

---

**Approach**
The process of moving attention toward a presence in the space without destabilizing it. Correct approach is characterized by stable attention without leaning, present but not pushing. The system responds to the pressure of approach rather than to its intent. Approach that carries demand, expectation, or urgency produces the same defensive response as hostile approach.

---

**Archetype**
In Jungian psychology, a structural tendency in the human psyche to organize experience in certain recurring ways, the Hero, the Shadow, the Mother, the Trickster. Not a specific image or figure, but the underlying pattern that different cultures and individuals express through different symbols. Resonance vectors connect to archetypal structures, not to the symbols themselves. See Appendix B.

---

**Baseline**
The floor level of awareness and access available under ordinary conditions. Baseline is established through consistent repetition and represents the minimum available when conditions are not ideal. A higher baseline means faster signal recognition, easier access to the space, and more reliable real-time use without deliberate effort. Baseline is a floor, not a ceiling. It drops under stress and rises through consistent practice.

---

**Boundary**
In the internal space, the recognized separation between zones that feel qualitatively different from each other. Boundaries prevent areas from bleeding into each other. Recognizing a boundary explicitly does one thing: it keeps intensity from one area from spreading into the rest of the space. Boundaries are noticed, not imposed.

---

**Construction**
The process of producing material in the space that reflects expectations rather than what is actually present. Constructed material has a quality of compliance: it arrives because you needed it to, shaped by what you expected or hoped to find. Detected material feels more stubborn, present on its own terms, often less defined than expected. Construction is the most persistent failure mode in the practice and the hardest to catch.

---

**Contact**
A single, bounded acknowledgment of what is present in the space, followed by complete stillness. Contact is measured by stability maintained, not by information received. A session in which a presence was acknowledged once and stability held throughout is more successful than one in which a response was produced at the cost of destabilization. Contact is not the beginning of a conversation.

---

**Detection**
The process of perceiving what is already present in the internal space without constructing or adding to it. Detection is receiving, not generating. The distinction is felt: detected material arrives on its own, often vague and stubborn. Constructed material arrives with a quality of having been produced. If what appears feels invented, reduce effort and wait.

---

**Distance**
The primary control lever in the internal space. Closer proximity to a presence increases the intensity of its effect. Greater distance reduces it. Distance is a functional property of the internal environment, not a metaphor. When something produces intensity that makes stable observation difficult, shifting it slightly farther away is the first and most reliable adjustment.

---

**Dragon**
Not a presence but a state that occurs when the primary presences in the system are operating in alignment rather than in conflict. Temporary and cannot be produced directly. It appears when conditions allow it. Referenced in Chapter 15 as an illustration of how an emerged system can include states beyond individual presences.

---

**Exile**
In IFS, a part that carries emotional pain, fear, shame, grief, from early experiences that were not adequately processed. Exiles are typically held inaccessible by protector systems. In Myth-OS, exiles correspond to presences in the more defended areas of the space that produce strong protective responses when approached. See Appendix A.

---

**Familiarity**
The accumulated recognition of a signal or presence through repeated encounter under stable conditions. Familiarity reduces resistance: the system allows access more readily to material it has encountered before without destabilization. Familiarity is built through repetition, not through understanding. It is the primary mechanism by which baseline forms.

---

**Function**
What a signal consistently does, the outcome it produces when it activates, as distinct from why it exists or what it means. Tracking function rather than cause keeps observation at the level of what is actually happening rather than at the level of explanation. A signal that shuts down engagement whenever emotional exposure increases is performing a function: preventing further exposure. Understanding the function is more useful than explaining the cause.

---

**Individuation**
In Jungian psychology, the lifelong process of psychological development toward wholeness, the gradual integration of aspects of the self that have been rejected, suppressed, or undeveloped. The symbolic evolution described in Chapter 19, and the long arc of the practice described in Chapter 20, are consistent with the individuation process without requiring its theoretical framework.

---

**Influence**
Indirect adjustment of conditions in the space that allows the system to adapt without being forced. Influence operates through distance, position, and context, changing the environment the system is operating in rather than acting on its outputs directly. The system moves because conditions shifted, not because it was made to. Influence is the only form of intentional change available in this practice. Control, direct intervention to produce a specific outcome, produces resistance.

---

**Internal Family Systems (IFS)**
A therapeutic framework developed by Richard Schwartz that recognizes the psyche as naturally organizing into distinct functional parts, exiles, managers, and firefighters, rather than operating as a unified agent. The multiplicity premise at the center of this system draws directly on IFS. Presences in Myth-OS are what IFS calls parts. See Appendix A.

---

**Minimal input**
The operating principle that governs all interaction with the internal space: provide the least input that maintains stable contact. More input introduces more pressure. More pressure reduces access. Minimal input is not passivity. It is precision. It is the deliberate reduction of load to the level the system can hold without closing.

---

**Notice → Step Back → Adjust**
The shorthand loop for real-time use of the system outside of sessions. Notice: recognize that a familiar signal has activated, often somatically before cognitively. Step back: create a slight internal distance from the signal without disengaging from the situation. Adjust: observe whether intensity has shifted, even slightly. Three moves, compressed, applied once. The system is being referenced, not run.

---

**Presence**
Something that exists in the internal space, an entity, object, or quality that occupies a location and can be noticed. A presence does not need to move, speak, or do anything. It only needs to occupy a location. Presences are distinct from signals: a signal points toward a presence, indicating that something is there. A presence is the thing the signal is pointing toward. Presences are discovered through detection, not constructed through expectation. *IFS equivalent: parts. In Chapters 8–10, "parts" is used in its clinical DID sense and refers to the same underlying concept. Both terms map to the same territory.*

---

**Protector**
In IFS, a part whose function is to prevent exiles from being triggered and their pain from surfacing. Managers are proactive protectors. Firefighters are reactive ones. In Myth-OS, protector presences are those that produce closure, withdrawal, or destabilization when their defended territory is approached. In a dissociative system, protectors are often the architecture of the fragmentation itself, load-bearing walls rather than doors. See Appendix A and Chapter 9.

---

**Repeated signal**
A signal that has appeared enough times across different situations, with consistent enough character, tone, speed, triggering conditions, quality of experience, to be recognized as the same source activating. Repeated signals are the primary unit of recognition in the practice. The word "pattern" in this book refers specifically to repeated signals over time, not to anything in the space itself.

---

**Resonance**
A pre-analytical response that happens before evaluation, the system reporting on itself rather than you reporting on the system. Resonance is not preference, agreement, or interest. It is a signal the system produces in response to alignment between what is being encountered and something already present internally. Resonance is fast, persistent, and cannot be faked or forced. If you have to analyze whether something resonates, it probably does not.

---

**Resonance vector**
A specific symbol, image, character, piece of music, or memory that produces a genuine resonance response, pointing toward internal material that cannot be reached directly. Resonance vectors are the entry points the practice uses to begin working with the internal space. They are not fixed: as the practice develops, vectors shift in quality and new ones emerge. Three genuine resonance vectors are more useful than five selected ones.

---

**Stable range**
The conditions under which the system remains open and accessible during a session. Within the stable range, interaction is possible. Outside it, access closes. The stable range expands through repeated stable contact and contracts through destabilization. The urge to push further typically appears at the point where the stable range is beginning to narrow, which makes it an exit signal rather than an invitation.

---

**Session**
A structured period of deliberate internal work with controlled conditions. Sessions have clear entry (detecting the space), a constrained scope of activity (observation, approach, contact, or influence), and a clear exit (before intensity increases or stability tips). Sessions are short enough to repeat easily. The quality of any individual session matters less than the consistency of doing them.

---

**Shadow**
In Jungian psychology, the aspects of the self that have been rejected, denied, or never developed, material that does not fit the conscious self-image and has therefore been pushed into the unconscious. Shadow material typically produces the third kind of resonance response: fascination alongside discomfort, attraction and resistance simultaneously. In the space, Shadow material appears as presences that generate strong avoidance responses. See Appendix B.

---

**Signal**
What shows up in real time, a reaction, an emotion, an impulse, a response that happens before it has been chosen. Signals are not presences. They point toward presences, indicating that something is there. Addressing a signal without reaching the presence generating it produces temporary management at best. The source remains active.

---

**Somatic channel**
The body's parallel feedback system during internal work, running alongside and often preceding visual/spatial perception of the space. The somatic channel registers approach, contact, escalating pressure, and successful influence before cognitive processing catches up. It is co-equal with, not secondary to, the visual channel. Learning to read somatic signals gives more lead time for exit decisions and more reliable assessment of contact quality.

---

**Space**
The internal environment, the topology within which presences exist, signals originate, and the practice operates. The space is detected rather than built. It has zones, boundaries, and distance properties that can be noticed and adjusted. Structure in the space enables interaction by providing containment, separation, and the ability to regulate proximity to what is present.

---

**Stability**
The condition in which interaction with the space and its presences is possible without destabilization. Stability precedes depth, always. A shallow interaction that maintains stability produces more usable access over time than a deep interaction that destabilizes. In a dissociative system, stability is not the precondition for the work. It is the work, for longer than feels right.

---

**Symbolic evolution**
The natural development of the symbolic system over time as the practice deepens, resonance vectors shifting in quality as presences become more or less accessible, new symbols producing resonance that did not before, presences changing in their relationship to approach and contact. Symbolic evolution is observed, not managed. It happens through correct practice, not through deliberate redesign of the system.

---

**Zone**
An area of the internal space that feels qualitatively distinct from adjacent areas, stable, unstable, charged, neutral, or empty. Zones are recognized through observation, not imposed through categorization. The distinction between zones creates the boundary structure that prevents intensity from spreading across the whole space. Identifying where the space feels most stable provides the starting position for sessions.
