# Submission Plan — Myth-OS

A staged plan from finished manuscript to signed book deal. Expect this to take 6–18 months end to end. Most of that time is waiting.

---

## Phase 0 — Pre-submission prep

**Estimated time: 2–4 weeks**

Before the first query goes out, the following pieces need to exist. Several already exist from earlier work. The rest can be built in this phase.

### Required

- [x] **Manuscript** — clean, line-edited, complete. Done.
- [ ] **Query letter** — see `query-letter-draft.md`. Personalize per agent.
- [ ] **Synopsis** — one-page (250 words) and three-page versions. Some agents want one, some want the other.
- [ ] **Book proposal** — required for nonfiction. Sections below.
- [ ] **Author bio** — one short version (50 words) and one longer (150 words).
- [ ] **Sample chapters** — typically the first 1–3 chapters, ready as a clean PDF or Word doc.

### Book proposal sections

Most agents and publishers expect a proposal that covers:

1. **Overview** — what the book is, the central premise, why it matters now. 1–2 pages.
2. **Audience** — who the book is for, sized concretely (people in IFS-style therapy, people doing trauma work, people with DID/OSDD, people who have exhausted insight-based approaches).
3. **Comp titles** — *The Body Keeps the Score*, *Self-Therapy* (Earley), *Waking the Tiger* (Levine), *It Didn't Start with You* (Wolynn), and 1–2 more recent titles. Each comp should include why your book is similar and how it is different.
4. **Author bio and platform** — credentials, lived expertise, where you publish or speak now, audience size if any, why you are the right person to write this book.
5. **Marketing and promotion** — what you bring (mailing list, social media, podcast, network), what events or media you plan, who has already endorsed or is likely to.
6. **Chapter outline** — annotated TOC. One paragraph per chapter.
7. **Sample chapters** — usually attached separately.

Two items from the manuscript repo to build on:

- The publisher-readiness report already names comp titles and the book's unique-position arguments. Use that material.
- The wiki you built can serve as the basis for a clean annotated TOC.

### Optional but useful

- **Endorsements / blurbs** — even one from a recognizable name (IFS clinician, trauma researcher) significantly increases agent interest. Worth pursuing in parallel with querying.
- **Platform evidence** — if any of the companion articles were published anywhere with measurable readership, include the numbers.
- **Sensitivity reader** — given the DID and complex trauma content, a reader with lived or clinical experience can confirm the framing is sound before submission.

## Phase 1 — Query round one

**Estimated time: 4–12 weeks (most waiting)**

Send to 8–12 Tier 1 agents in the first batch. Track everything in a spreadsheet.

### Approach

- **Personalize every query.** Reference one specific thing the agent has placed or said they want. Form queries get form rejections.
- **Stagger send dates** by a few days so responses do not all arrive in the same week.
- **Do not query the same agency twice.** Most agencies have a one-query-at-a-time rule across all their agents.
- **Follow each agent's submission guidelines exactly.** If they want query only, send query only. If they want query plus first ten pages, send that. Deviations get auto-deleted.
- **Use email subject line conventions** they specify, or "Query: MYTH-OS (75,000 words / Self-Help & Trauma)" as default.

### Response types

- **Form rejection or no response within 8–12 weeks.** Move on. Most queries get no reply.
- **Partial request** — agent asks for sample chapters or proposal. Respond within 24 hours with the materials, polite cover note, manuscript status.
- **Full request** — agent asks for the full manuscript. Respond within 24 hours. This is the strongest possible response short of an offer.
- **Offer of representation** — go to Phase 2.

### When to send round two

After 8 weeks of round one, if no offers and at least half the agents have either passed or gone silent, start round two with the next batch.

---

## Phase 2 — Evaluating an offer of representation

**Estimated time: 1–2 weeks per offer**

An offer triggers two things:

1. **Notify every other agent currently considering the manuscript.** Standard practice. Gives them a deadline (usually 1–2 weeks) to make their own decision.
2. **Vet the offering agent thoroughly.**

### Vetting checklist

- Recent sales: pull their Publishers Marketplace deal history. They should have placed at least 2–3 books in the last 18 months in your category.
- Client list: who else do they represent? Are those authors getting good deals?
- Talk to current clients if possible. Ask: do they return emails, do they go to bat in negotiations, do they help shape the book or only sell it.
- Contract: agent contract terms (commission rate, term, termination clause). 15% domestic / 20% foreign is standard.
- The conversation: get on a phone call. The agent should articulate a clear vision for the book and a specific submission strategy. If they cannot name editors they would pitch, that is a red flag.

If multiple offers come in, pick the agent whose vision for the book matches what you want it to be, not the one with the biggest name.

## Phase 3 — Publisher submission via agent

**Estimated time: 2–6 months from signed agent agreement to book deal**

Once you have an agent:

- They revise the proposal with you. Expect 2–4 weeks of polishing before submission.
- They send to editors in tiers — usually 5–10 editors in the first round, more if needed.
- Editors respond on their own timeline. 4–8 weeks per round is typical.
- An offer triggers an "auction" or a "preempt" depending on interest level. Your agent runs this.

What you do during this phase: nothing related to submission. Work on the next thing.

---

## Phase 4 — If no agent after rounds one and two

**Decision point at month 4–6**

If you have queried 20–30 agents and not received a representation offer, the next moves depend on what feedback you got:

- **No partial requests at all** → the query letter or premise is not landing. Revise the query letter, possibly the comp titles, and try a different batch.
- **Partial requests but no full requests** → the sample chapters are not converting interest. Consider whether the opening is doing what it needs to do.
- **Full requests but no offers** → the book itself is being read but not bought. This is the hardest case. May require a developmental edit, may require waiting for a different cultural moment, may require trying direct-to-publisher.

### Direct-to-publisher path

Targets: Sounds True, North Atlantic Books, New Harbinger. These accept unagented submissions and publish in this space.

Process: tailor the proposal slightly to each one's stated interests. Send. Wait. Same response cycle as agents but with smaller volumes.

The trade-off: smaller advance, smaller marketing budget, smaller bookstore footprint, but real distribution, real editorial work, and real publication credit. For a book like this, the editorial fit at Sounds True or North Atlantic may be better than a mid-tier Big Five imprint anyway.

---

## Phase 5 — Hybrid or independent path

**Decision point at month 12–18 if nothing has worked**

If traditional and direct-to-publisher both fail, the question becomes: do you want the book to exist?

If yes:

- **Hybrid press**: Greenleaf, She Writes / SparkPress, Lioncrest. Cost: $5K–$25K. You retain rights and most royalties. You get editorial and design support. You do not get traditional bookstore placement.
- **Independent**: KDP for ebook and paperback, IngramSpark for wider paperback distribution, ACX for audio. Cost: minimal. You do all the work.

Either path is reasonable if the goal is to make the system available to people who need it. Neither is equivalent to a Penguin Life advance and the marketing that comes with it.

## Tracking

A simple spreadsheet (or Notion table, or whatever you prefer) with columns:

| Agent | Agency | Date sent | Material | Personalization | Response | Date | Notes |

Update it the day you send and the day you hear back. Six months in you will not remember who you queried otherwise.

## What to work on during the waiting

- Build the platform you wish you had. Substack, longer-form essays drawing on the manuscript, podcast appearances if you can get them.
- The companion articles (1–10) are publishable as a series. Pitching them to mental health publications or running them on Substack builds the audience and the credibility before the book lands.
- Stay quiet about the book status publicly until there is something to announce.
