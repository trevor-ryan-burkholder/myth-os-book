# Query Letter Draft — Myth-OS

A first-pass draft, annotated below. Personalize the opening and the agent-specific paragraph for every submission.

---

## Draft

**Subject:** Query: MYTH-OS (78,000 words / Self-Help & Psychology)

Dear [Agent first name],

[Personalized opening — 1–2 sentences. See the customization notes below.]

Most people who do serious internal work arrive at the same frustration: they understand exactly why they do what they do, and they still do it. Insight does not produce change. The frameworks that explain a reaction do not interrupt it. MYTH-OS is a 78,000-word practical guide to closing that gap.

The book describes a system for working with internal states the mind does not allow direct access to. It draws on Internal Family Systems, Jungian psychology, and narrative therapy, but it is not a synthesis of them. It is a functional method, calibrated for use without a clinician in the room, that addresses what most self-help in this space avoids: the structural reason direct effort fails, and what to do instead. Five chapters cover the conditions of working within a dissociative system, material that is rarely treated with this level of practical specificity outside clinical literature.

Comparable titles include Bessel van der Kolk's *The Body Keeps the Score* (Viking, 2014) and Mark Wolynn's *It Didn't Start with You* (Viking, 2016) for the somatic and intergenerational trauma framing, and Jay Earley's *Self-Therapy* (Pattern System Books, 2009) for the practical, between-session orientation. MYTH-OS fits between those: more procedural than van der Kolk and Wolynn, more accessible to the general reader than Earley.

I am not a clinician. I am someone who needed this to work and made it work. I have Dissociative Identity Disorder, Complex PTSD, and Bipolar 1 with psychotic features, and the system in this book was developed across years of working with my own internal material under conditions where adequate clinical support was not always available. That context is the credential I am offering. The book is direct about it.

The manuscript is complete and has been through four editing passes. A 10-part companion essay series, drawing the same material into shorter form for general readers, is also complete. I would be glad to send the full proposal, sample chapters, or the manuscript at your request.

Thank you for your time.

Sincerely,

Trevor Ryan Burkholder
[email]
[city, state]
[website or social if any]

## Annotations

### Subject line

Most agents specify a format. If they do not, use: `Query: TITLE (word count / category)`. This signals professionalism and saves them from having to open the email to know what it is.

### Personalized opening

The first line should prove you have done your research. Pick one of:

- A book the agent represented that connects directly: *I'm reaching out because you represented [Author] for [Book]. The way that book treats [specific thing] is the closest reference point for what mine is trying to do.*
- A stated interest from their #MSWL: *Your most recent post on Manuscript Wish List mentioned [specific interest]. MYTH-OS sits in that territory.*
- An interview or panel they did: *In your recent Publishers Weekly piece on trauma-informed nonfiction, you mentioned [X]. That is the gap MYTH-OS tries to address.*

Generic openings ("I am querying you because you represent self-help") read as form letters and get treated as form letters.

### Hook paragraph

The current opening leads with the universal problem (the gap between knowing and changing), not with the book. This is deliberate. Agents read hundreds of queries a week. The hook needs to make them feel the problem before it announces the solution.

**Alternative hook** if you want a more system-forward opening:

> MYTH-OS is a practical operating manual for the part of the mind insight cannot reach. The book describes a system, calibrated across years of working with a dissociative internal system, for accessing material the protective structures of the mind do not let in through direct effort.

The first version is reader-centric. The second is book-centric. Test both.

### Body paragraph

Three things this paragraph has to do:

1. Say what the book is. (A practical system.)
2. Position it relative to the field. (Draws on IFS, Jungian, narrative therapy, but is its own method.)
3. Name what makes it different. (The DID chapters, the procedural specificity, the operability outside clinical contexts.)

Keep it tight. Agents skim.

### Comp titles

Two to three recent (within 10 years if possible) traditionally published comp titles. The current draft uses older comps because they are the genuine reference points; van der Kolk and Wolynn remain in print and dominant in the category, which is fine. If you can find one trade comp from the last 3–5 years that sits in the same territory, add it. Possibilities:

- Janina Fisher, *Healing the Fragmented Selves of Trauma Survivors* (Routledge, 2017) — more clinical, but a category reference.
- Resmaa Menakem, *My Grandmother's Hands* (Central Recovery Press, 2017) — somatic, trauma, narrative.
- Stephanie Foo, *What My Bones Know* (Ballantine, 2022) — complex trauma memoir-with-research.
- Richard Schwartz, *No Bad Parts* (Sounds True, 2021) — IFS for general readers. Strong direct comp.

**No Bad Parts** is probably your closest current comp. Strongly consider including it.

### Bio paragraph

This is the paragraph that decides whether an agent takes the book seriously. The current draft does three things:

1. Names the disclosure factually, without apology or melodrama.
2. Frames it as the source of the book's authority, not a problem to be solved.
3. Closes with the credential argument explicitly: "That context is the credential I am offering."

If you have any platform — published essays, Substack subscribers, podcast appearances, speaking engagements, a clinically credentialed endorser — add one sentence about it before the closing sentence. If you have none yet, leave it as is. Honesty is better than inflation. Agents can tell.

### Length

Current draft is ~330 words in the body plus signature. Standard query length is 250–400 words. This is within range.

## Variant — narrative nonfiction angle

For agents whose list leans more literary than self-help, lead with the personal angle and reframe the book as a practitioner-authored field guide rather than as a self-help book.

> Dear [Agent],
>
> [Personalized opening.]
>
> I have Dissociative Identity Disorder, Complex PTSD, and Bipolar 1 with psychotic features. Across years of doing trauma work without consistent access to clinical support, I built a method for interacting with my own internal material that worked when nothing else did. MYTH-OS is the book that describes it.
>
> The method draws on Internal Family Systems, Jungian psychology, and narrative therapy, but it is not a synthesis or a memoir. It is a functional system: a sequence of moves with constraints, calibrated for use outside the therapy room, that addresses why direct cognitive effort almost always fails to change what it has correctly diagnosed. Five chapters cover the specific conditions of working within a dissociative system, material that does not appear in this kind of practical detail outside clinical literature.
>
> [Comp titles paragraph as above.]
>
> [Standard close.]

This version leads with credential-via-experience rather than with the reader's frustration. Use it for agents whose stated interests skew toward memoir, narrative nonfiction with strong voice, or lived-experience authorship.

## Customization checklist before sending

- [ ] Agent name spelled correctly. Verified against their current agency page (people move).
- [ ] Personalized opening references something specific to this agent.
- [ ] Their submission guidelines followed exactly (query only vs. query + pages vs. query + proposal).
- [ ] Subject line in their requested format if they specify one.
- [ ] Your contact information accurate.
- [ ] Attachments only if requested. Many agents reject queries with unsolicited attachments unread.
- [ ] One last pass for typos. A typo in a query about a heavily edited book reads worse than usual.
- [ ] Date and agent name logged in your tracking spreadsheet before you click send.

## What not to do

- Do not query an entire agency at once. Most have a one-agent-at-a-time rule. Violating it can blacklist you across the agency.
- Do not query an agent who explicitly says they are closed to queries. They will not make an exception.
- Do not follow up before 12 weeks have passed unless they specified a different response window.
- Do not include the manuscript in the initial query unless they specifically asked for it.
- Do not make claims about market size, viral potential, or comparable sales figures unless you can substantiate them.
- Do not apologize, soft-pedal, or hedge about the disclosure. The book's authority depends on its directness about it. The query has to match.
