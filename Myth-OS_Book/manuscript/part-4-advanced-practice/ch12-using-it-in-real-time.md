# Chapter 12 — Using It in Real Time

Sessions are controlled. You choose when to enter, how long to stay, when to exit. You set the variables.

Real life does not work like that. A conversation escalates before you notice anything is happening. A reaction starts running before you identify the signal. By the time you catch what is occurring, you are already inside it.

That is not failure. That is the condition the system has to work in.

---

**The standard**

Real-time use will not be clean. Stop measuring it against sessions and it starts being useful.

In a session, you observe from a stable position, make light contact, exit cleanly. In a real situation, you often notice a signal after it has already moved you. You catch it mid-reaction, or only as the intensity drops. That still counts. Noticing what happened ten seconds ago is not noticing in advance, but it is not nothing. It shortens the gap between activation and awareness. The gap closes through repetition.

Partial improvement is the standard. Not elimination. Reduction. A reaction at seventy percent produces different outcomes than one at full force, even if both are still reactions. The goal is not to stop reacting. The goal is a small amount of space between the signal and its full expression.

---

**The loop**

Three moves: **notice, step back, adjust.**

**Notice.** A familiar signal has activated. Same tone, same speed, same quality you have seen before. Recognition is enough. Do not analyze. Do not name the presence. *This is that signal.* That is the whole step.

The body usually beats the mind here. A tightening in a specific location, a change in breathing, a forward pressure or withdrawal that is characteristic of this signal. Reading the body's early registration gives you more time than waiting for cognitive recognition. Notice is often a somatic notice first.

**Step back.** A small internal distance from the signal. Not disengaging from the situation. Not suppressing the response. Not trying to make it stop. The same distance adjustment you practice in the space, compressed. The signal is still active. You are still in the room. Your position relative to the signal has shifted by a small amount.

Somatically: a slight settling. A single exhale a little longer than the last. A momentary attending to feet on the floor, weight in the chair. Something that registers the body as distinct from the signal rather than identical with it.

**Adjust.** Notice whether the shift changed anything. Not a dramatic change. Slightly lower intensity. Slightly more room. The situation a little more manageable than it was two seconds ago. If yes, that is the result. If no, the situation continues and you have lost nothing.

---

**What it looks like**

You are in a conversation and something the other person says produces an immediate tightening in the chest. You recognize it. You are already responding before you catch it consciously. But the body registered it first, and that registration gives you two seconds you would not otherwise have had. You create a small amount of internal distance. The response continues, slightly less at full force. You say something you might still regret. Not the version you would have said without the adjustment.

That is a valid result.

You are in a situation that is escalating. Familiar rate, familiar physical location, signals you have tracked before. You do not stop the escalation. You exhale. Feet on the floor. Step back internally by a small amount. The escalation may continue. You have more information about what is happening.

That is a valid result.

You are already deep in a reaction. Minutes in, not seconds. You recognize what has been running. *This is that signal.* The naming does not undo what happened. It shortens the aftermath, and it adds a data point that makes earlier recognition more likely next time.

That is also a valid result.

---

**Real-time use in a dissociative system**

If you have a dissociative system, real-time use has an extra variable. You may not be the same part in the middle of a difficult situation that you were when you built familiarity with the signal.

A signal one part recognizes early may run unrecognized in another. A part that has not practiced may now be running things because the situation triggered a switch. That is not a failure of the practice. It is what a dissociative system does under pressure.

The adjustment is a second question added to notice. After *this is that signal*, ask *which part am I right now, and does this part have access to the practice.* Not analytically. An orienting check. A brief attending to whether the current state has familiarity with internal work or is operating without that reference.

When a part is present that does not have access to the practice, the full loop is not available. The somatic step still is. A single grounding move. The exhale. Physical contact with ground. No cognitive recognition layer required. It is not optimal. It is what is available in that state, and it introduces a small amount of space.

Developing the practice across more parts is a longer-term goal. It happens through the same mechanism as everything else. Repeated stable contact over time, building familiarity in parts that did not previously have it.

---

**The common failures**

The most common failure is treating real time like a session. You notice a signal and try to run the full process. Locate the space. Find the presence. Establish distance. Maintain stability. That sequence requires conditions that do not exist in a real situation. Attempting it mid-conversation adds cognitive load at the moment the system is already under pressure. The signal intensifies. The situation moves forward without you. You end up more reactive than if you had done nothing.

Less, not more. Notice somatically if you can, cognitively if not. Step back by the smallest usable amount. Observe what changes. Three moves, applied once. The system is not being run in real time. It is being referenced. The sessions are the training. Real time is the application of what the training made available.

The other failure is expecting full control. A signal that has been activating for years will not be overridden by months of session work and a moment of awareness. What happens is that the activation runs slightly differently than it would have. Slightly less automatic. Slightly more visible. Slightly more room between the signal and the response. That difference accumulates. It is not visible in any single instance. Across repeated situations it produces real change.

The measure is whether the gap shortened. Whether the response produced slightly less damage than it would have without the adjustment.

By that measure, late recognition is still recognition.
