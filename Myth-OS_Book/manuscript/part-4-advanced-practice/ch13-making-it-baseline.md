# Chapter 13 — Making It Baseline

The system starts as something you do deliberately. You set conditions, enter the space, observe what is present, apply what you have learned in a controlled sequence. Each step requires a decision. The process is effortful in the way that any new skill is effortful, not because it is complicated, but because nothing about it is yet automatic.

That changes through repetition. Not through increased effort, not through better technique, but through the simple accumulation of the same sequence run enough times that the sequence stops requiring full conscious activation. What was new becomes recognizable. What was recognizable becomes expected. What was expected begins to happen before it is called.

---

**The mechanism**

Repetition builds familiarity with specific signals, their tone, their speed, the conditions that trigger them, the quality they carry when they arrive. That familiarity reduces the processing required to identify them. A signal you have encountered fifty times registers differently than one you have encountered twice. The fifty-times signal arrives already partially labeled. You do not have to work backward from the reaction to identify what is happening. The recognition is faster because the system has already done most of that work.

Familiarity also reduces resistance. A signal that has been approached repeatedly in stable conditions, observed without force, engaged without pressure, adjusted without demand, carries less defensive charge than one that has never been approached directly. The system has recorded that contact with this signal does not destabilize anything. Resistance exists to prevent destabilization. When the record shows that destabilization has not occurred across repeated contact, resistance decreases. Detection becomes easier not because you have gotten better at it but because the system is less actively preventing it.

Easier detection means lower effort. What required deliberate attention begins to happen with less input. The signal registers before you have consciously turned toward it. The recognition arrives with the activation rather than after it. And because less effort is going into detection, more is available for what comes next, the small adjustment, the slight increase in distance, the shift in position that changes how the signal runs.

---

**The markers**

The markers of this shift are not dramatic, but they are consistent once you know what to look for.

Detection becomes faster. A signal that used to take minutes to identify, after the reaction had already run most of its course, now registers within seconds of activation. You are not ahead of it, but you are much less behind it. In a conversation that begins to press on something familiar, you notice the signal arriving rather than noticing, afterward, that it had arrived. The difference between those two is not mastery. It is a shorter gap, and a shorter gap is enough to change what is possible.

Effort decreases. In the early stages, entering the space required preparation, quieting the environment, settling attention, deliberately lowering the pressure that direct access produces. Over time that preparation shortens. The space becomes accessible with less setup because the access route has been traveled enough times that it does not need to be re-established from scratch each session. The same applies to real-time use. The step back happens because it has happened enough times before, not because you decided to initiate it this time.

Intensity reduces. Not because the signals change, and not because the presences generating them have been resolved. Because repeated stable contact with a signal changes how the signal behaves under contact. A signal that has been approached without force, observed without demand, and adjusted without suppression has a different activation profile than one that has only ever been met with resistance or overwhelm. The peak is lower. The duration is shorter. The aftermath is lighter.

Somatically, baseline formation has a recognizable quality that develops over time. Early in the practice, entering the space requires deliberate settling, a conscious reduction of the forward pressure that direct attention tends to carry. As the baseline develops, that settling begins to happen on its own. The body moves toward the space's register rather than having to be brought there. The physical preparation that used to take several minutes begins to take seconds, then becomes unnecessary. That shift in the body is one of the clearest markers that the practice has moved from deliberate to baseline, not a cognitive assessment but a physical one.

---

**Realistic framing**

None of this is constant. The system does not stay in the same accessible state regardless of circumstances. Under high stress, with insufficient sleep, in situations that involve material the system has not yet worked with directly, detection slows and effort rises. The baseline is a floor, not a ceiling. It represents the minimum level of awareness available under ordinary conditions, not the maximum available under ideal ones.

Baseline awareness runs quietly in the background under ordinary conditions, becomes less reliable under pressure, and requires deliberate reactivation when something new or more charged is being encountered. That is not a limitation of the system. It is an accurate description of how any integrated skill behaves. The chess player who can read the board automatically in familiar positions still has to slow down and think deliberately in unfamiliar ones.

---

**In a dissociative system**

Baseline formation in a dissociative system is not a single process. It is multiple processes, happening at different rates in different parts, often without coordination between them.

A part that has been practicing consistently for months may have genuine baseline access: the space is familiar, signals are recognized early, the somatic settling happens quickly. Another part that is rarely present during practice sessions may have no baseline access at all: the space is unfamiliar, signals are unrecognized, the practice has to be rebuilt from the beginning each time that part encounters it.

This means the baseline you experience is part-specific. The floor is not the same across the whole system. It is the floor available to the part currently present, which varies. On days when a practicing part is running things, the baseline is more accessible. On days when a less-practiced part is present, it may not be available at all.

The practical implication is that baseline formation in a dissociative system requires extending the practice to more parts over time, not forcing participation, but creating conditions where less-practiced parts gradually develop their own familiarity. The resonance vectors are useful here again: a piece of music that produces a consistent response across states can serve as a cross-state anchor that begins to build familiarity even in parts that are not actively practicing. The somatic grounding moves from the real-time loop are similarly cross-state transferable: the body responds to them before the part present has cognitive access to the practice.

The other implication is that regression in baseline access is more common and less meaningful in a dissociative system than in an integrated one. A day when the floor seems to have dropped significantly may be a day when a less-practiced part is more active, not a sign that the practice has been undone. Holding that distinction prevents the discouragement that comes from interpreting state-specific variation as systemic regression.

---

**The failure mode**

The failure mode at this stage is trying to maintain the awareness actively. You notice that detection has become easier and decide to sustain that by monitoring more carefully, watching for signals throughout the day, running the loop consciously in situations that do not require it, staying alert in a way that resembles vigilance rather than awareness. That effort produces the opposite of what it is trying to preserve. Passive detection works because it is not being driven. When you start driving it, it requires the same deliberate activation it required at the beginning.

The marker that the system is becoming baseline is that you notice things without having tried to notice them. If you are trying to notice, the system has not yet made that move. Both are correct positions to be in: the system develops through the trying stage before it reaches the automatic stage. Knowing which stage you are in prevents the error of treating one as the other.

---

Go through a day without running the system deliberately. Do not enter the space. Do not look for signals. Do not apply the loop. At the end of the day, notice whether any of the following occurred without deliberate initiation: a signal registered faster than it would have previously, a reaction ran lighter or shorter than expected, you found yourself aware of what was happening mid-situation rather than only in retrospect. If any of those occurred, the system produced them without being asked. That is the marker. If none occurred, the system continues developing through the work already in place. Either outcome is correct.

What you are looking for is not evidence that the system is working perfectly. You are looking for evidence that it is working without you having to run it.

What required effort begins to happen with less input. That is what baseline formation looks like from the inside.
