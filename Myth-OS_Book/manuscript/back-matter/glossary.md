# Glossary

---

**Access**
Indirect interaction with protected internal states. Never direct retrieval. Built through repetition and indirect approach, not through force or insight. Understanding something is not the same as having access to what is generating it.

---

**Acknowledgment**
The only permitted form of direct input during contact. A single internal statement, *I know you're there*, said once. No follow-up. It is a statement of awareness, not the opening of a dialogue. Anything beyond it adds pressure.

---

**Approach**
Moving attention toward a [[presence]] without destabilizing it. Stable attention without leaning. The system responds to the pressure of approach, not the intent behind it. Approach that carries demand produces the same defensive response as hostile approach.

---

**Archetype**
In Jungian psychology, a structural tendency in the psyche to organize experience in recurring patterns: the Hero, the Shadow, the Mother, the Trickster. Not an image. The pattern beneath the images. [[resonance-vector]]s connect to archetypal structures, not to the symbols themselves. See Appendix B.

---

**Baseline**
The floor level of awareness and access available under ordinary conditions. Built through consistent repetition. Drops under stress, rises through practice. A floor, not a ceiling.

---

**Boundary**
The recognized separation between [[zone]]s that feel qualitatively different. Boundaries keep intensity from one area from bleeding into the rest of the space. Noticed, not imposed.

---

**Construction**
Producing material in the space that reflects expectation rather than what is present. Constructed material has a quality of compliance. Detected material is more stubborn, often less defined than expected. The most persistent failure mode in the practice.

---

**Contact**
A single bounded acknowledgment of what is present, followed by complete stillness. Measured by stability maintained, not by information received. Not the beginning of a conversation.

---

**Defenses**
Mechanisms that block direct access to protected material. Activated by pressure, force, or premature exposure. They do not evaluate intent. They read one thing: forward force. They respond by closing.

---

**Detection**
Perceiving what is already in the space without adding to it. Receiving, not generating. Detected material arrives on its own, often vague and stubborn. If what appears feels invented, reduce effort and wait.

---

**Distance**
The primary control lever in the space. Closer proximity increases intensity. Greater distance reduces it. A functional property, not a metaphor. When intensity makes stable observation hard, shifting slightly farther is the first adjustment.

---

**Dragon**
Not a presence. A state that occurs when the primary presences in the system are operating in alignment rather than in conflict. Temporary. Cannot be produced directly. Referenced in Chapter 15.

---

**Exile**
In IFS, a part that carries pain, fear, shame, grief, from experiences that were not adequately processed. Held inaccessible by protector systems. In Myth-OS, exiles correspond to presences in the more defended areas of the space. See Appendix A.

---

**Familiarity**
Accumulated recognition of a signal or presence through repeated stable encounter. Familiarity reduces resistance. Built through repetition, not through understanding. The primary mechanism by which [[baseline]] forms.

---

**Function**
What a signal consistently does. The outcome it produces when it activates. Tracking function rather than cause keeps observation on what is happening, not on explanation.

---

**Individuation**
In Jungian psychology, the lifelong process of integrating what has been rejected, suppressed, or undeveloped. The symbolic evolution in Chapter 19 and the long arc in Chapter 20 are consistent with individuation without requiring its theoretical frame.

---

**Influence**
Indirect adjustment of conditions that lets the system adapt without being forced. Operates through distance, position, and context. The only form of intentional change available in this practice. Control produces resistance.

---

**Inner Topology**
The internal landscape where presences exist and interaction occurs. The manuscript uses [[the-space]].

---

**Internal Family Systems (IFS)**
A framework developed by Richard Schwartz that treats the psyche as naturally organizing into distinct functional parts, exiles, managers, and firefighters. The multiplicity premise at the center of this system draws directly on IFS. [[presence]]s in Myth-OS are what IFS calls parts. See Appendix A.

---

**Minimal input**
The operating principle: provide the least input that maintains stable contact. More input adds pressure. More pressure reduces access. Not passivity. Precision.

---

**Notice → Step Back → Adjust**
The shorthand loop for real-time use outside sessions. Notice: a familiar signal has activated. Step back: create slight internal distance without disengaging. Adjust: check whether intensity has shifted. Three moves, compressed, applied once.

---

**Presence**
An internal figure, part, or agent. Equivalent to *parts* in IFS. Something that exists in the space, occupies a location, and can be noticed. A presence does not have to move, speak, or do anything. It only has to be there. *IFS equivalent: parts. In Chapters 8–10, "parts" is used in its clinical DID sense and refers to the same underlying concept.*

---

**Protector**
In IFS, a part whose function is to prevent exiles from being triggered. Managers are proactive. Firefighters are reactive. In Myth-OS, protector presences produce closure, withdrawal, or destabilization when their defended territory is approached. In a dissociative system, protectors are often the architecture of the fragmentation itself. See Appendix A and Chapter 9.

---

**Repeated signal**
A signal that has appeared enough times across different situations, with consistent enough character, to be recognized as the same source activating. The primary unit of recognition in the practice. *Pattern* in this book refers to repeated signals over time, not to anything in the space itself.

---

**Resonance**
The emotional signal that indicates relevance. Used to locate internal material without forcing access. Pre-analytical: the system reporting on itself rather than you reporting on the system. Fast, persistent, not fakeable. If you have to analyze whether something resonates, it probably does not.

---

**Resonance vector**
A specific symbol, image, character, piece of music, or memory that produces a genuine resonance response. The entry points the practice uses. Not fixed. Three genuine vectors are more useful than five selected ones.

---

**Session**
A structured period of deliberate internal work with controlled conditions. Clear entry, constrained scope, clear exit. Short enough to repeat easily. Consistency matters more than any single session.

---

**Shadow**
In Jungian psychology, aspects of the self that have been rejected, denied, or never developed. Shadow material typically produces fascination alongside discomfort. In the space, it appears as presences that generate strong avoidance responses. See Appendix B.

---

**Signal**
What shows up in real time. A reaction, an emotion, an impulse, a response that happens before it has been chosen. Signals are not presences. They point toward presences. Addressing the signal without reaching the presence produces temporary management at best.

---

**Somatic channel**
The body's parallel feedback system during internal work, running alongside and often preceding visual-spatial perception of the space. Co-equal with the visual channel, not secondary to it. Often the faster channel.

---

**Space**
The internal environment. The topology within which presences exist, signals originate, and the practice operates. Detected, not built. Has zones, boundaries, and distance properties that can be noticed and adjusted.

---

**Stability**
The condition in which interaction with the space is possible without destabilization. Stability precedes depth, always. A shallow interaction that stays stable produces more usable access over time than a deep one that destabilizes. In a dissociative system, stability is the work, for longer than feels right.

---

**Stable range**
The window of arousal within which the system can function. Not baseline calm. The functional zone. Expands through repeated stable contact. Contracts through destabilization. The urge to push further usually appears at the point where it is beginning to narrow.

---

**Symbolic evolution**
The natural development of the symbolic system as the practice deepens. Resonance vectors shift. New symbols produce resonance that did not before. Observed, not managed.

---

**Symbolic Interface Layer**
The system component through which symbols, images, and narrative structures function as the working surface for accessing protected material. The route resonance operates through.

---

**Translation**
The conversion of internal work into real-world behavior. Internal change that does not translate into external life does not complete the system.

---

**Zone**
An area of the space that feels qualitatively distinct from adjacent areas. Stable, unstable, charged, neutral, empty. Recognized through observation, not imposed through categorization. The distinction between zones creates the boundary structure that keeps intensity from spreading.
