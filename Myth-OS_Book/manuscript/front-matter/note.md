# Author's Note

My name is Trevor Ryan Burkholder and I am deeply traumatized.

Here are the greatest hits:

- Physically assaulted six times before the age of thirteen
- Put in a cardboard box and had fireworks thrown in at me
- Kidnapped and left deep in the woods by older kids
- Almost drowned by another kid in a pool
- Forced to fight
- A rock thrown into my eye that permanently damaged it
- No real friends before fifteen
- Near-complete social exclusion until the same age
- Constant bullying until I physically stood up for myself junior year of high school
- Constant parental conflict
- Parentified by both parents

That's just before the age of fifteen.

I have attempted to take my own life multiple times since then.

There is more. I won't go into it here.

The point is: if you've been through it, I've probably been through something similar. I have friends who've had it worse.

These experiences, combined with genetics and several head injuries, left me with a specific set of diagnoses: Bipolar 1 with psychotic features, Complex PTSD, and Dissociative Identity Disorder. Plus the depression and anxiety that attend all of those. I am currently on seven psychiatric medications.

For a long time the question I kept returning to was a simple one: what is the point of all of it.

This book is the point.

---

I spent years trying to do what most people try to do with trauma and psychological fragmentation. I analyzed it. I journaled about it. I built frameworks for understanding it. I got better at describing what was happening without being able to change any of it. The gap between knowing and changing, between understanding a reaction and being able to interrupt it, stayed exactly where it was regardless of how precisely I could articulate it.

What eventually worked was something different. Not confronting the material directly but learning to approach it indirectly. Not forcing access but building the conditions under which access became possible. Not understanding the system but learning to interact with it on its own terms.

I have DID. That means my internal world is not unified. It is a system of distinct presences, each with their own function, their own responses, their own relationship to the material we all carry. Working with that system required developing an approach that could tolerate fragmentation, that did not depend on continuous memory between sessions, that could hold multiple presences without collapsing them into a single narrative. The constraints that make this system work for a dissociative mind turn out to make it work for any mind, because the underlying mechanism, that protected material resists direct access and responds to indirect approach, is not specific to DID. It is how psychological protection works.

The system in this book has roots in Carl Jung's work, in Internal Family Systems therapy, and in narrative therapy. None of it is original. What is original is the specific combination, the particular constraints, and the procedural form, turning a set of clinical frameworks into something that can be practiced independently, between therapy appointments or instead of them, by someone working alone with their own internal material.

I am not a clinician. I am someone who needed this to work and made it work. That is the credential I am offering.

---

This book is for people who have hit the limits of insight-based approaches. People who know exactly why they do what they do and still do it. People doing self-directed IFS, Jungian work, or trauma processing, with or without a therapist. People whose internal worlds feel fragmented, defended, or simply unreachable through direct effort.

If that is you, keep reading.

The system works. Not because I designed it well, but because I needed it to, and I had enough material to test it on.
