# Chapter 10 — Stability as the Primary Goal

Everything in Part 2 treats stability as a precondition for the work. You establish it first so that deeper access becomes possible. Stability is the foundation. The work happens on top of it.

In a dissociative system, that framing needs to be revised.

Stability is not the precondition for the work. Stability is the work, for longer than feels right, and to a degree that an integrated system does not require. Not because the deeper material is less important, but because in a fragmented system the deeper material is structurally inaccessible until the baseline holding the whole system together is solid enough to tolerate contact with it. Trying to move to depth before that baseline is established does not accelerate the process. It destabilizes the foundation and makes the baseline harder to rebuild.

I learned this the hard way, in a period early in my practice when I pushed consistently past the stable range because I could feel that there was significant material available and I wanted to reach it. What I got instead was increased switching, extended periods of destabilization between sessions, flooding that was difficult to contain, and a system that became more defended over the following weeks rather than more accessible. The material I was trying to reach moved further away, not closer. The protectors tightened. The space became less stable, not more.

The work that reversed that was not depth work. It was weeks of nothing but short sessions focused entirely on stabilizing the space, entering, observing, making no contact, exiting cleanly. Boring, unremarkable sessions that felt like they were accomplishing nothing. What they were accomplishing was rebuilding the baseline that the earlier overreach had eroded.

---

**What baseline stability means in a dissociative system**

In an integrated system, baseline stability means a consistent ability to enter the space, observe what is present, and exit cleanly without destabilization. It is a state of the system that you maintain between sessions and build through consistent practice.

In a dissociative system, baseline stability has an additional dimension: it means the system as a whole, across parts, not only in the part currently conducting sessions, is not in a state of active crisis, significant flooding, or escalating internal conflict.

This is not always knowable from the inside. One of the features of DID is that significant distress can be occurring in parts of the system that are not currently active, with the part currently running daily life relatively unaware of it. The signals that indicate systemic instability, increased switching, unusual reactivity in daily life, sleep disruption, somatic symptoms without obvious cause, a quality of internal noise that does not have a clear source, are often the only visible indicators that something is happening in parts of the system you are not currently in contact with.

When those signals are present, the practice should stop or contract. Not because the signals make the practice dangerous in principle, but because the system as a whole does not have the stability to absorb the additional activation that internal work produces. The sessions that feel most important during these periods, when there is a quality of urgency about reaching what is present, are the sessions most likely to make the situation worse.

I have a rule I have held for several years: when I notice increased switching, I stop internal work sessions for at least three days and do only stabilization practices, grounding, routines, external engagement. No sessions. No resonance work. Nothing that activates the internal space. Baseline maintenance only, until the switching reduces. It is a difficult rule to follow when the urgency is high. It is the right rule.

---

**Stabilization practices**

Stabilization in a dissociative system is not only what happens during sessions. It is what happens between them, how you move through daily life in a way that supports the system's overall coherence rather than fragmenting it further.

A few things that function as stabilization across states:

**Consistent external structure.** Routine is stabilizing for a dissociative system in a way it may not be for an integrated one. When the environment is predictable, parts that carry threat-sensitivity are less activated. When it is unpredictable, those parts activate more readily, which increases switching and reduces the system's overall capacity for internal work. This is not about rigidity, it is about reducing the activation load the system is carrying so that internal work sessions do not exceed the available capacity.

**Grounding practices that reach across states.** Some grounding practices are state-specific: they work for the part that learned them but not reliably for other parts. The most useful stabilization practices for a dissociative system are ones that operate at a level that does not depend on which part is active: consistent sensory inputs, physical routines, environmental anchors that multiple parts recognize. Resonance vectors can function this way: a piece of music that produces a settling response across states is a cross-state stabilization resource.

**Communication between parts where it is available.** Not every dissociative system has internal communication between parts. Some do, at varying levels of reliability. Where it exists, using it to establish shared understanding that internal work is happening, that the sessions are intentional, that the space is being worked with deliberately, can reduce the alarm response that some parts have to internal work conducted by other parts. A part that does not know why the system is entering the internal space may experience the activation as threat. A part that knows what is happening may be less alarmed by it.

In my system, I have a few parts with whom I have developed reliable communication. When I plan to do a session, I give those parts a brief heads-up, not a detailed explanation, only an acknowledgment that internal work is about to happen and a signal that I will exit if anything escalates. This is not always necessary and not always possible. When it is possible, it reduces the systemic disruption of the sessions.

---

**Assessing whether the system is stable enough to proceed**

Before any session, particularly in periods when systemic stability is uncertain, a brief pre-session check is useful. Not elaborate, a few seconds of attending to the system as a whole rather than to the space specifically.

The questions are simple: Is there a quality of internal noise or conflict that is unusual? Is switching elevated above its normal baseline? Is the body carrying a level of activation that is higher than typical for this time of day and these external circumstances? Is there a quality of urgency about the session, a feeling that something important must be reached today, that has the character of pressure rather than genuine readiness?

If the answer to any of these is yes, the session is either shortened significantly or postponed. The urgency in particular is worth treating as a stop signal. In my experience, urgency about reaching specific material is almost always a sign that the system is already activated around that material, which means the session is starting from a more destabilized position than it appears, and the stable range is narrower than usual.

The sessions that feel most urgent are the sessions that require the most caution. This is counterintuitive and it remains true regardless of how many times I have verified it.

---

**What progress looks like in a dissociative system**

In an integrated system, progress is incremental but relatively linear. Access deepens over time. Presences become more familiar. The stable range expands. There is a general direction of development even if individual sessions vary.

In a dissociative system, progress is not linear. There are periods of genuine development followed by periods of regression, periods where the system contracts under external pressure, periods where material that was accessible becomes inaccessible again as the system reorganizes around new information or new stress. This is not failure. It is the nature of a system that is managing more complexity than an integrated one.

What progress looks like across a longer arc: the systemic baseline gradually becomes more robust. Periods of destabilization become shorter and less severe. The system's overall capacity to tolerate internal work increases slowly over time, with regular contractions. Parts that previously had no access to the practice begin to develop some. Protectors that previously closed immediately on approach allow slightly longer observation windows. The internal space becomes more consistently recognizable across states.

None of this happens quickly. The timeline for significant development in a dissociative system is longer than most frameworks suggest, not months, but years. This is not a pessimistic assessment. It is an accurate one, and holding it accurately prevents the chronic overreach that comes from expecting faster progress than the system can sustain.

The practice in this book was developed and tested across years of working with a dissociative system. The conservatism of its constraints, the emphasis on stability, the insistence on early exits, the repeated instruction not to force, reflect that timeline honestly. Not as a warning, but as a description of what the actual work looks like when it is being done correctly.

Slow is not the same as stuck. It is the pace at which this particular kind of work can be done without continuously undoing what was built.
