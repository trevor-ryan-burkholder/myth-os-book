# PART 3 — FRAGMENTED SYSTEMS

# Chapter 8 — When the System Is Fragmented

Part 2 assumes continuity. You enter, observe, make contact, exit, and come back the next day to something you recognize. The space holds. The presences hold. Yesterday is available today.

For some of us, none of that is true.

Not because the practice broke. Because the system was never organized around continuity. It was organized around separation.

This part is for those systems.

---

**What a fragmented system looks like**

I have Dissociative Identity Disorder. My internal system is not one structure. It is a set of distinct parts, each carrying different material, different memories, different ways of operating. Some are aware of each other. Some are not. What I find in the space in one state is not the same space I find in another.

This is not the loose sense of different versions of yourself showing up in different rooms. It is structural. Parts of my system do not share information with other parts. A session done in one state may be unavailable to the part doing the next one. A presence I worked with carefully for weeks can be unknown to whichever part shows up on a bad day.

If this is your situation, you already know it. You may have a label, OSDD or DID, or you may have severe dissociative responses that never got a clinical name. The label matters less than the structure. Your system does not run as a single thing, and Part 2 needs to be held with that in mind.

If this is not your situation, Part 2 was enough. This part will inform. The constraints in it are not yours.

---

**What changes and what does not**

The principles of Part 2 do not change. Indirect access. Stability before depth. Minimal input. No forcing. These hold harder in a fragmented system, not softer. The cost of violating them is faster and more total. Switch. Further fragmentation. Flooding that does not contain.

What changes is the relationship between sessions.

In an integrated system, sessions stack. In a dissociative system, they may not. Different parts may run different sessions. The part present today may not have what the part present last week encountered. This is not failure. It is structure.

The practical implications:

**Each session may start from zero.** Not because nothing happened before. Because the part present may have no access to what happened before. The opening moves, detecting the space, locating structure, checking what is present, are not formalities. They are genuine first contact, performed by a part that may be encountering the space for the first time.

**The space changes shape across states.** Mine does. Some parts access it as a structured environment with familiar zones. Others find it darker, chaotic, harder to navigate. Some have no access at all. The space is simply not there in that state. This is normal. It tells you which parts have built familiarity with the space and which have not.

**Presences may be strangers to the part working.** A presence carefully encountered over months by one part can be unknown to the part conducting today's session. The approach principles still apply. The accumulated familiarity does not transfer. Treat it as first contact.

---

**Continuity across states**

Continuity is not automatic. It is not impossible either. It has to be built.

Keep brief external records. Not detailed analysis. Analysis pulls the work back into the head. Brief notes: what part was present, what part of the space was available, what was encountered, what the body did. These notes are available across states when internal memory is not. Read them at the start of a session. Briefly. Do not rely on them.

I keep a simple log. Date. Which part. What the space felt like. Whether anything was there. I do not build a narrative from it. I use it like a map someone else drew. Orientation, not instruction.

Resonance vectors function as cross-state anchors. A piece of music that produces a strong response in one state often produces a response in others, even when those states share no memory of using it. Resonance is not stored in episodic memory. It sits at a level the system does not fully separate. A resonance vector at the start of a session can orient toward the space when the part present has no direct access to what came before.

Not every part will engage. Some parts of a dissociative system are not interested in this work, have no access to the space, or are actively against internal work. Do not force them. This system works with willing parts. A part that is not willing right now is not a problem. It is a condition.

---

**The additional safety requirement**

In an integrated system, the stability principles function as good practice. In a dissociative system, they function as structural requirements.

When I push past the stable range, when I stay too long, push for clarity the system is not offering, force contact with a presence that is not ready, the consequence is not only a harder next session. It is a switch. A shift to a part less stable, less equipped, or carrying the material the overreach activated. Flooding that lands in an integrated system over hours can land in a dissociative one in minutes, because the parts holding vulnerable material are structurally closer to the surface.

The exit criteria in Chapter 7 are not guidelines here. They are stops.

- Rising intensity: exit.
- Clarity dropping: exit.
- Body signaling escalating pressure: exit.

The urge to push further arrives earlier in a dissociative system and is a less reliable signal of genuine opportunity. Treat it as a warning either way.

The hours after a session also require attention. A switch after a session means the session exceeded what the current part could hold. If you notice a state change in the hours that follow, a different quality of feeling, dislocation, the sense that someone else is running things, note it. That is information about where the actual limit was. Reduce the next session accordingly.

---

**What this part is for**

The next two chapters address protector systems inside fragmentation, and what baseline stability looks like when the system runs in discrete states. Both require what is set here: the fragmentation is structural, Part 2 still applies but with less margin, and the patience required operates at a different scale than integrated work uses.

This system was built inside a dissociative system. The conservatism, the early exits, the minimal input, the instruction to leave before the boundary rather than after, comes from the price of not doing it. Not in theory. In sessions that went wrong. In switches and flooding and the work of putting the system back together after.

The constraints are not overcautious. They are calibrated to the actual conditions. If your system is fragmented, they are calibrated to yours.
