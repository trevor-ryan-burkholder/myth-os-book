# Chapter 18 — What Actually Changes

Most people start this expecting something to go away.

The signals that cause the most trouble will stop activating. The reactions that wreck the most situations will be replaced by better ones. The presences that generate the most intensity will resolve and stop mattering.

That expectation is wrong. Getting clear on what does and does not change is necessary for running the system correctly over time.

The signals do not go away. The presences stay in the space. Hard situations keep happening. Reactions still fire, sometimes at full strength, sometimes at the worst possible moment. The system does not remove any of this.

What it changes is narrower. Your position relative to what is happening, and what that position makes available.

---

**Position**

Position is not a metaphor. It refers to how much a signal is running you automatically versus how much separation you have from it.

At the start, the signal activates and the response follows immediately. There is no gap. Signal and reaction are a single event. You are inside the reaction before awareness registers it.

What the system builds, through repeated stable contact, is a small amount of space between those two things. The signal still activates. The reaction still runs. But there is a point, brief, partial, sometimes only seconds wide, where the activation is visible before the reaction completes. That point is where something else becomes possible. Not control. Not override. Not a better response replacing the automatic one. Only the gap where previously there was none.

The signal still happens. You are no longer fully inside it.

The body learns this before the mind names it. Early in the practice, the activation and the reaction arrive as one physical state. Later, the body starts registering the activation as slightly distinct from what it produces. A second or two opens up where the signal is present but the full somatic state of the reaction has not yet arrived. That second is the gap made physical.

---

**Short-term markers**

Earlier detection comes first. A signal that used to register only after the reaction had completed starts registering closer to activation. The reaction may still finish. The recognition arrived ahead of it rather than behind it, which changes what is available in the moment. A few seconds of lead time is a real change in what that moment contains.

Reduced intensity comes second. Reactions still occur. They feel less consuming, not because the signal produces less charge, but because some of it is being observed rather than experienced. The peak is lower. The duration is shorter. The aftermath is lighter.

Response flexibility comes third. When a gap exists between signal and completion, that gap contains the possibility of a slightly different choice. Not a considered decision. A small variation in how the reaction expresses. You still react. You react differently, some of the time. In a situation that would have produced a sharp response, what appears is a slightly less sharp one. The difference between one option and two is significant from the inside, even when the second is only marginally better than the first.

---

**What a year looks like**

Short-term markers show up within weeks to months. The longer arc is different in character. Less about individual sessions. More about what has shifted in the underlying structure.

At six months, the most frequently encountered signals are detectable early enough that real-time use is more reliable. Not consistent. Reliable. The gap appears often enough to stop being a surprise. Baseline formation has begun in the most-practiced areas. The space is accessible with less preparation. Contact that required careful setup six months ago is available with less overhead.

What has not changed at six months: signals still activate at full strength. The most defended presences are still defended. New situations the practice has not engaged produce the same automatic responses they always did. The work generalizes only to material that has been directly contacted.

At one year, something structural has shifted. The baseline has stabilized at a higher floor. Returning to the practice after a gap, a week, two weeks, a hard period, no longer requires rebuilding from the beginning. What was built persists more durably than it did in the early months, when a few days were enough to reset progress. The system has, to some degree, internalized the practice.

The presences worked with consistently across that year have changed in their relationship to approach. Not resolved. Still there. Still generating signals. Still in their locations. The stable range for observing them has expanded. What required five sessions to approach carefully a year ago can be approached more directly now. Not because the presences changed. Because the accumulated record of stable contact reduced the defensive charge around them.

What has not changed at one year: the most protected material is still protected. Presences carrying the most significant early trauma remain heavily defended in most systems. A year builds access to accessible material and starts creating conditions for approaching more defended material. It does not reach the most defended directly. That timeline is longer, and for many people requires clinical support alongside the independent practice.

---

**What becomes permanent and what stays effortful**

Some things become automatic with enough repetition. Signal recognition in familiar situations. The somatic grounding moves of the real-time loop. Basic access to the space at the start of sessions. These stop requiring deliberate activation because they have happened enough times.

Some things never become automatic because they should not. Approach to heavily defended presences requires deliberate pacing regardless of accumulated practice. The material is still protected. The protection exists for real reasons. Checking whether a session exceeded the stable range stays deliberate, not because the skill is undeveloped but because the assessment should always be conscious. Exit decisions benefit from staying deliberate.

The difference between what becomes automatic and what stays effortful is not about skill. It is about the nature of the task. Routine access to familiar material can be automatic. Contact with protected material should remain deliberate. The deliberateness is the mechanism that keeps the contact safe.

---

**In a dissociative system**

The long arc in a dissociative system is longer and less linear. A year of practice looks different here, not because less work has happened, but because the work is distributed across multiple parts with different access and different relationships to the practice.

What a year typically produces: more parts with some familiarity with the practice. A higher systemic floor even on difficult days. Protectors that previously closed immediately on approach allowing slightly longer observation windows. Internal communication between parts somewhat clearer, where it exists at all. Switching that was previously unrelated to sessions may track to recognizable triggers that can be anticipated.

What a year does not typically produce: resolution of the deepest fragmentation. Access to the most protected material. A unified experience of the practice across all parts. The timeline for that work is longer, often many years, at a pace the system sets.

---

**Calibrating expectations**

The failure mode at this stage is expecting changes that match the size of the effort. Months of consistent work producing a reaction that runs forty minutes instead of an hour does not feel proportionate. It produces the conclusion that the system is not working, or that the changes are too small to matter, or that a different approach would work faster.

Small shifts in position compound across repeated activations. A reaction that runs slightly shorter each time is a reaction producing slightly less damage across the situations it appears in. A gap that is two seconds wide in one instance and five in another is a gap that is widening. The changes are not dramatic in any single instance because they are not supposed to be. They are cumulative. Their scale is visible only across time.

Measuring against resolution will always disappoint. Resolution is not what is being built. What is being built is a different relationship to what happens. The situations are the same. The signals are the same. What changes is how much of the automatic response you are inside versus how much you can observe.

The progress check, three questions, after any recent situation involving a familiar signal:

Did the signal register earlier than it would have.

Was the intensity slightly lower, or the duration slightly shorter.

Was there any moment of separation, any point where the signal was visible before the reaction had completed.

If any of these has shifted, even marginally, the system is working.

What changes is not the signal. What changes is where you are standing when it activates, and how much of it arrives with your full unobserved participation versus how much of it you can see while it happens.

That shift, accumulated across time, is what the system produces. Not transformation. Not resolution. A different relationship to what is there. More space. More visibility. More options available in moments that previously had none.

That is what changes. Not a new self. A different position relative to the one already here.
