# Approach

Moving attention toward a [[presence]] in [[the-space]] without destabilizing it.

Correct approach is stable attention, present but not leaning. The system reads the pressure of approach, not its intent. Curiosity produces the same defensive response as hostility if both are applied with force. Demand does not require aggression. It is present any time you expect something to happen, wait for a response, or try to make what you see clearer than it currently is.

Expectation is the most common source of invisible pressure. You notice something and hold the expectation that it will shift, respond, reveal itself further. That expectation changes how you attend. You are no longer observing. You are waiting for a result, which is demand. The [[presence]] responds to the demand. What was visible becomes less defined, or disappears.

Approach requires a particular quality of attention: present but not leaning. Your position stays fixed. If the [[presence]] shifts location, you do not follow it. If it becomes less clear, you do not sharpen it. You hold and observe what remains. The [[somatic-channel]] often registers leaning before the mind does: a forward quality in the chest, a pulling sensation, a change in breathing. Catching it there gives you room to hold the approach correctly.

In a [[working-with-a-fragmented-system|fragmented system]], approach starts further back than feels necessary. Movement toward a defended [[protector]] begins from a distance that produces no response at all.
