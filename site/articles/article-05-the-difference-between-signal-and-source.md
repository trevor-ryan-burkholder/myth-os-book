You are in a conversation and something shifts. A tightening in the chest. A sudden urge to leave. A response forming before you have decided to respond. You are already moving before you know why.

Or you are pulled in two directions simultaneously, one part of you moving toward something while another part resists. Not confusion exactly. More like two separate positions being held at once, neither of them fully yours, both of them running.

Or someone says something ordinary and you react as if it weren't ordinary at all. The reaction is disproportionate and you know it is disproportionate and that knowledge changes nothing about the reaction.

These are signals. They are what shows up in real time, reactions, emotions, impulses, responses that happen before you have chosen them. They are the most visible part of the system. They are also not the part worth focusing on.

---

A signal points toward something. It does not replace the thing it is pointing toward.

In the internal environment described in the previous piece, there are presences, elements that occupy locations, that exist whether or not you are attending to them, that generate signals in daily life the way a radio tower generates transmission. The signal is what reaches you. The presence is what is sending it.

This distinction is not semantic. It has direct practical consequences for why most approaches to internal work stall at the same place.

When a signal activates, the response is immediate and reasonable: address the signal. Understand it, regulate it, reframe it, suppress it, work with it in session. All of that effort lands on the signal. The presence generating it remains exactly where it was, doing exactly what it has always done. The signal is managed temporarily. The source stays active. The same signal generates again from the same place, on the same schedule, under the same conditions.

You are not failing to work hard enough. You are working on the wrong thing.

---

You have already encountered this distinction outside of any formal practice.

The reaction that arrives faster than thought, before you have formed an opinion about the situation, before you have decided how you feel, is not coming from your analysis. It is coming from somewhere else. Something that already has a position on the situation, that has had a position on situations like this one for a long time, that does not wait for your input before responding.

The conflicting pulls, wanting to move toward something while another part of you resists, feeling two incompatible things simultaneously without being confused, are not a sign of ambivalence or disorder. They are two distinct sources generating two distinct signals. Each one is pointing toward something separate. The conflict you experience is real because the sources are real and they genuinely have different positions.

The version of yourself that appears under specific conditions, under stress, in certain relationships, in situations that press on particular material, and behaves in ways the rest of you would not choose. That version is not a lapse. It is a presence operating with its own logic, its own responses, its own relationship to the situation. The signal it generates is recognizable. The source of it is what has not yet been reached.

---

The observation that the psyche organizes into distinct functional elements is not new. Internal Family Systems therapy is built on it. Jungian psychology is built on it. Most serious depth psychology frameworks arrive at the same place through different routes: the mind is not a single unified thing. It is a system of distinct parts, each with its own function, its own responses, its own relationship to the material the whole system carries.

I am not asking you to adopt any of those frameworks. I am asking you to notice what you have already directly experienced: that the conflicting pulls are real, that the reactions arriving faster than thought are coming from somewhere, that the versions of yourself appearing under specific conditions are not random. The multiplicity you notice in yourself is not confusion or disorder.

It is structure.

---

Here is why this matters practically.

A signal that is managed without reaching the presence generating it produces temporary relief at best. The signal quiets. The source remains active. When conditions shift, when pressure increases, when a similar situation recurs, when the system encounters something that activates the same material, the signal returns at the same strength. Sometimes higher, because the management created pressure that the source registered and responded to.

This is the mechanism behind the experience of working on something for years and finding it unchanged. The work was real. It landed on the signal. The presence generating the signal was never contacted. It continued doing what it was organized to do, indifferent to the work happening at the surface.

Reaching the source requires a different move than addressing the signal. Not confrontation. That produces the same defensive response described in the second piece. Something more like approach. Careful, patient, low-pressure approach to something that exists in a specific location in the internal environment and can be perceived there once you know what to look for.

---

Return to the internal space from the previous piece.

Ask once, internally: *Is there anything here?*

Then observe without engaging. Do not move toward what you notice. Do not ask it questions. Do not try to determine what it is or what it represents.

What you are looking for is a presence, not a signal, not a feeling about the space, but something that occupies a location within it. It may not have a clear form. It may feel like a quality in a particular area, a sense that one part of the space is different from the rest in a way that is not merely structural. It may be vague enough that you are not sure whether you are noticing it or imagining it. That uncertainty is normal and does not need to be resolved.

Alongside whatever appears visually, notice the body. As you ask whether anything is there, what happens physically? A shift in the chest or the belly? A change in breathing quality? A sense of weight appearing somewhere that was not weighted before? The body registers the presence of what is in the space in parallel with whatever imagery arises. Both are reading the same thing.

If something is present, notice its location. Notice what quality it carries, stable, reactive, neutral, charged. Notice what the body does in response to its proximity. Then leave it there. Do not approach it yet. Do not try to make contact.

If nothing is present, that is also valid information about current conditions. The space may not show what is there on the first visit. It shows what conditions currently allow.

---

Recognition is the only goal of this step.

Not definition, not understanding, not interaction. You are confirming that something exists in a specific location within a structured space, and that you can be near it without disrupting it.

The signal is what you have been working with. The presence is what has been generating it. The gap between those two things is where every previous approach has stopped.

The next piece is about what happens when you begin to close that gap, and why the instinct that feels most natural in that moment is the one most likely to shut everything down.
