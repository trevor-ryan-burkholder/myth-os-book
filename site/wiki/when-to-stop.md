The system is designed to be used independently. The design is intentional: the stability-first approach, the conservative constraints, the emphasis on [Minimal input](/wiki/minimal-input/) and early exits, calibrated for self-directed practice without clinical oversight. For most people, in most situations, it is safe to use that way.

There are conditions under which it is not. The system works within a [stable range](/wiki/stable-range/). When the baseline is destabilized beyond a certain point, the stable range shrinks to where internal work becomes counterproductive. Recognizing that limit is part of using the system correctly, not a concession to it.

---

## Conditions that require stopping

**Active suicidal ideation.** Not reduced internal work, not more careful internal work. Stopped. The activation that sessions produce in a system that is already at that level of distress is not useful. It adds load to a system that is already beyond its capacity. Crisis support comes first.

**Psychotic episodes, active hallucinations, significant breaks from reality.** States where the boundary between the internal space and external reality is unclear require psychiatric evaluation before any internal work continues. The internal environment this system works with is distinct from psychotic experience, but the distinction requires clinical assessment to navigate safely. If you are uncertain whether what you are experiencing falls into this category, that uncertainty is itself a reason to stop and seek professional consultation.

**Significant dissociative escalation.** Switching that is more frequent or more disruptive than the typical [Baseline](/wiki/baseline/), extended periods of depersonalization, amnesia outside the normal range, identity confusion more severe than usual. This is particularly relevant for people with DID or OSDD. The Part 3 chapters describe working within a dissociative system during ordinary periods. They do not describe working within a dissociative system that is actively destabilizing. The latter requires stabilization before internal work resumes.

**Active substance use that affects stable observation.** The system depends on the ability to hold a position and observe without being pulled in. Substances that alter that capacity alter the fundamental operating condition of the practice.

**Extended periods of acute external crisis.** When daily life is genuinely unstable, when basic functioning is compromised, when the external environment is demanding full resources, depth internal work does not produce useful results. Stabilization practices (grounding, routine, external support) are appropriate. Sessions aimed at deeper access are not.

---

## The practical rule

If baseline stability outside of sessions is significantly compromised, stop internal work sessions. Get stabilized through whatever means are available, professional support, crisis resources, changes in external circumstances. The practice will still be here.

---

## Stopping is not failure

It is accurate reading of the conditions. The practice continues when the conditions for it are present. When they are not, recognizing that is the most useful thing you can do.
